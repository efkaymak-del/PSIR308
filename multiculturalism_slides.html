Where We Are

Over the past eleven weeks we have traced a chain of corrections: utilitarianism's aggregation problem led to Rawls's difference principle; Rawls's insensitivity to choice led to Dworkin's auction; libertarians challenged redistribution entirely; Marxists challenged the liberal property framework; citizenship theorists demanded active civic virtue; feminists exposed the public/private divide; and critical theorists — de Beauvoir and Fanon — argued that liberal categories cannot see pre-political subordination.

Today we ask: can liberalism accommodate cultural difference? Kymlicka says yes — with modifications. Benhabib says: not so fast.

The Central Question

"A liberal democracy's commitment to individual freedom and equality seems to require identical treatment of all citizens, regardless of group membership. But members of minority cultures argue that identical treatment can itself be a source of injustice."
— Kymlicka, Chapter 8

This is the multiculturalism paradox: treating everyone the same may require treating some people differently.

Part I

Kymlicka's Framework

Societal culture, group-differentiated rights, and the liberal case for minority protection

Societal Culture

Kymlicka's argument begins with a concept he borrows and extends: societal culture — "a culture which provides its members with meaningful ways of life across the full range of human activities, including social, educational, religious, recreational, and economic life, encompassing both public and private spheres" (Kymlicka, 76).

Individual autonomy — the capacity to form, revise, and pursue a conception of the good — requires a context of choice. That context is a societal culture. Without it, freedom is empty.

This is Kymlicka's liberal move: cultural membership is not opposed to individual freedom — it is a precondition of it.

Two Kinds of Cultural Diversity

National Minorities

Previously self-governing, territorially concentrated cultures that have been incorporated into a larger state — often involuntarily. They seek to maintain themselves as distinct societal cultures.

Examples: Québécois in Canada, Catalans in Spain, Kurds in the Middle East, Māori in New Zealand, Indigenous nations in the Americas.

Polyethnic / Immigrant Groups

Individuals and families who have voluntarily left their national community to join another society. They typically seek integration into the existing societal culture — but on terms that respect their identity.

Examples: Muslim communities in France, Spanish-speaking immigrants in the US, South Asian communities in the UK.

Three Types of Group-Differentiated Rights

1. Self-government rights — Delegation of political authority to national minorities, typically through federalism or territorial autonomy. The strongest claim: a group demands its own political unit.
2. Polyethnic rights — Legal protections and positive measures for immigrant groups to express their cultural particularity: religious exemptions, funding for cultural festivals, anti-discrimination laws, mother-tongue services.
3. Special representation rights — Guaranteed seats or quotas in legislative bodies for underrepresented groups, ensuring their voice in collective decision-making.

Note: these are not mutually exclusive. A single group may claim more than one type.

The Liberal Limit: Internal Restrictions vs External Protections

External Protections ✓

Rights that protect a minority group from the decisions of the larger society — shielding them from economic or political domination. These are consistent with liberal principles.

Example: Québec's language laws requiring French in workplaces protect the francophone culture from anglophone economic dominance.

Internal Restrictions ✗

Rules imposed by a group on its own members to prevent internal dissent or deviation. These restrict individual freedom within the group and are inconsistent with liberalism.

Example: A community that forbids its members from marrying outside the group or leaving the religion.

Kymlicka's line: liberalism supports external protections but opposes internal restrictions. A group can demand protection from the majority but cannot oppress its own members.

Discussion — 3 minutes

Québec's Bill 21 bans public-sector employees from wearing religious symbols. Is this an external protection (defending Québec's secular culture from religious fragmentation) or an internal restriction (suppressing the rights of religious minorities within Québec)?
Think about this from Kymlicka's framework. Can a national minority use its self-government powers to impose restrictions on sub-minorities?
Part II

Problems with Kymlicka

Where the framework strains, bends, and breaks

Problem 1: The Binary Is Too Neat

Kymlicka's framework rests on a clean distinction between national minorities and polyethnic immigrants. But many groups fit neither category:

Roma in Europe — not territorial, not immigrants, centuries-old presence, no demand for self-government. Neither category applies.
African Americans — not a national minority in Kymlicka's territorial sense, not voluntary immigrants. Their presence is the product of enslavement.
Maronites of Cyprus — an indigenous community since the twelfth century, forced by the 1960 constitution into the Greek Cypriot community. Not immigrants, not a national minority with territorial claims. Constitutionally invisible.

If the framework cannot classify these groups, what use is it?

Problem 2: Culture Is Not a Box

Kymlicka treats 'societal culture' as a relatively stable, bounded entity — something you belong to, like a container. But cultures are not containers. They overlap, shift, hybridise, and conflict internally.

"Cultures are not sealed packages but ongoing processes of negotiation, contestation, and transformation."

A Catalan university student in Barcelona participates in Spanish civic culture, Catalan linguistic and political culture, European institutional culture, and global digital culture — simultaneously. A Kurdish journalist in Istanbul navigates Turkish civic life, Kurdish linguistic identity, Islamic culture, and transnational media. Which is their 'societal culture'? Kymlicka's framework wants a single answer where real lives give many.

This is where Benhabib enters.

Problem 3: The Liberal-Democratic Precondition

Kymlicka's entire framework assumes a functioning liberal-democratic state that recognises rights, tolerates dissent, and provides institutional channels for minority claims. But even where those institutions exist, they can fail — and where they are weakening, the framework has nothing to offer:

Catalans in Spain — a national minority with a distinct language, an elected parliament, and EU membership. Yet when Catalonia held a self-determination referendum in 2017, Madrid suspended autonomy under Article 155 and imprisoned elected leaders. The liberal-democratic channels Kymlicka assumes were available — and were shut down.
Kurds across the Middle East — a national minority of over 30 million with no recognised state. In contexts of democratic backsliding, Kurdish parties have been closed, elected officials imprisoned, and language rights curtailed. Where liberal institutions are eroding, Kymlicka's framework loses its footing entirely.
Uyghurs in China — the claim is not for group-differentiated rights but for basic cultural survival under mass detention and forced assimilation.

Kymlicka's theory describes what a just liberal state should do. It has much less to say about what happens when the state itself is the problem — whether in a consolidated democracy, a backsliding one, or an authoritarian regime.

Part III

Benhabib's Correction

From static categories to democratic iteration

Benhabib Against the "Mosaic" Model

Seyla Benhabib argues that theorists like Kymlicka treat cultures as if they were tiles in a mosaic — clearly bounded, internally uniform, externally distinct. She calls this the "reductionist sociology of culture".

The problem: when you grant rights to a 'culture,' you must first decide what that culture is, who speaks for it, and where its boundaries lie. This inevitably empowers conservative elites within the group — the people who claim to represent the 'authentic' culture — and silences dissidents, women, and reformers.

"The right to cultural self-expression should not become the right of cultural elites to police their members."
— Benhabib, The Claims of Culture

Benhabib's Three Normative Conditions

Benhabib does not reject group rights outright. She argues that any cultural accommodation must satisfy three conditions:

1. Egalitarian reciprocity — Members of a cultural group must not receive fewer rights than members of the majority. Cultural practices that subordinate women, children, or dissenters fail this test.
2. Voluntary self-ascription — Individuals must be free to define their own relationship to their culture. No one should be assigned a cultural identity by the state or by community leaders against their will.
3. Freedom of exit and association — Members must be genuinely free to leave the group without facing material ruin, social ostracism, or violence. Exit must be a real option, not merely a formal one.

Democratic Iteration

Benhabib's alternative to Kymlicka's fixed categories is democratic iteration — an ongoing process in which universal norms (human rights, equality, dignity) are reinterpreted and renegotiated through public deliberation in specific local contexts.

Cultural meanings are not fixed. They are contested, revised, and transformed through democratic engagement. The French headscarf debate, for example, is not a clash between 'French culture' and 'Islamic culture' — it is an ongoing democratic argument about what secularism, equality, and religious freedom mean in a pluralist republic.

The key insight: cultural boundaries are politically constructed and can be politically reconstructed — but only if the three conditions above are met.

Discussion — 5 minutes

Apply Benhabib's three conditions to the Maronites of Cyprus. The 1960 constitution forced them to choose between the Greek or Turkish community. Does this satisfy voluntary self-ascription? Is freedom of exit meaningful when there are only two options and neither is yours?
Think about what democratic iteration would look like in the Cyprus context. Who would participate? What would be renegotiated?

Cases 1 & 2 — National Minorities

Québec, Catalonia, and the Kurds

Québec is Kymlicka's strongest case: a national minority with self-government rights within a liberal federation. Bill 21 is the complication — Québec uses those very powers to restrict religious minorities within its borders. Kymlicka's framework can identify this as an internal restriction, but can it stop it?

Catalonia and the Kurds present the same classification — textbook national minorities with distinct languages, territorial bases, and historical self-governance claims — but radically different institutional contexts. In Spain, a consolidated EU democracy, an independence referendum was met with criminal prosecution and the suspension of autonomy. In contexts of democratic backsliding, Kurdish parties have been closed, elected officials imprisoned, and language education curtailed for decades. Kymlicka's framework can classify both groups easily — but it cannot explain why institutional channels fail in both a consolidated and a weakening democracy.

Common thread: Kymlicka's categories work for classification but struggle with enforcement — whether the state imposes internal restrictions (Québec), criminalises self-determination claims (Catalonia), or denies recognition outright (Kurds).

Cases 3 & 4 — Polyethnic Claims

French Hijab Ban and US English-Only Order

France frames laïcité as a universal civic principle. Kymlicka would classify French Muslims as a polyethnic group entitled to polyethnic rights — including religious expression. But France rejects the very category of polyethnic rights, insisting on undifferentiated republican citizenship. Whose liberalism wins?

Trump's Executive Order 14224 (March 2025) designated English as the sole official language, revoking multilingual federal services. The affected communities are classic polyethnic immigrants — no territorial claim, no self-government demand, just access to public services. Kymlicka would support their polyethnic rights. The state disagrees.

Common thread: both cases show liberal states actively restricting polyethnic accommodation in the name of national unity.

Case 5 — The Category Breaker

Roma in the EU

The Roma have lived in Europe for centuries. They are not immigrants. They do not claim territory. They do not seek self-government. They have no 'kin state.' They are systematically excluded, impoverished, and segregated across multiple EU member states despite decades of policy frameworks.

Kymlicka's binary — national minority or polyethnic immigrant — simply cannot classify them. This is not a marginal exception; it is 6 million people falling through the theoretical floor.

Benhabib's framework handles them better: the question is not which category they belong to but whether egalitarian reciprocity, voluntary self-ascription, and freedom of exit are being met. The answer, across virtually every EU member state, is no.

Case 6 — Beyond Liberalism

Uyghur Cultural Suppression

The Uyghurs are, in Kymlicka's terms, a textbook national minority: territorially concentrated, linguistically distinct, historically self-governing. His framework would grant them self-government rights, polyethnic protections, and special representation.

None of this matters. China is not a liberal democracy. There is no constitutional channel, no rights framework, no institutional mechanism through which Uyghur claims can be advanced. The claim has collapsed from group-differentiated rights to basic human survival.

This is the hardest test: Kymlicka's theory presupposes the very conditions that are absent. Benhabib's democratic iteration also requires a functioning public sphere — which does not exist in Xinjiang.

Case 7 — The Closest to Home

The Maronites of Cyprus

The 1960 Constitution recognised only two communities. Maronites, Armenians, and Latins were classified as 'religious groups' and forced to choose one. The Maronites — present since the crusades, with their own language, their own church, their own villages — became a sub-category of the Greek Cypriot community. Their parliamentary representative cannot vote.

Scholar Andrekos Varnava calls this 'internal exclusion': the bi-communal framework does not just ignore diversity — it structurally erases it. The constitutional architecture has room for two peoples and only two.

Kymlicka would likely classify the Maronites as a national minority. But the constitution does not recognise that category. And Benhabib's voluntary self-ascription was violated at the very founding of the state, when Maronites were compelled to choose an identity that was not theirs.

Discussion — 5 minutes

Look at the seven cases together. In how many of them does Kymlicka's framework successfully identify the problem? In how many does it offer a solution? What does the gap between diagnosis and remedy tell us about the limits of liberal multiculturalism?

The Chain of Corrections — Updated

Kymlicka extended liberal equality to cultural membership: if autonomy requires a context of choice, then protecting minority cultures is a liberal obligation. This was a genuine advance over colour-blind liberalism.

But his framework is too categorical (the binary breaks), too static (cultures change), and too optimistic (it assumes liberal states will cooperate). Benhabib corrects each of these: she replaces fixed categories with normative conditions, replaces the mosaic with democratic iteration, and insists on individual agency within cultural contexts.

Neither framework fully succeeds. The seven cases we examined today show that the real world generates forms of cultural injustice that outrun even the best liberal theory. That is not a reason to abandon theory — it is a reason to keep correcting it.
