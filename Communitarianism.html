PSIR 308 · Contemporary Political Philosophy · Week 10

Communitarianism

Kymlicka, Chapter 6

The self is not prior to its ends — or is it?

Context

The semester so far

Every theory we've studied shares what Dworkin calls the egalitarian plateau — the conviction that the interests of each person matter, and matter equally.

They disagree on what those interests are and how to treat them equally.

Last week, Beauvoir and Fanon asked: who counts as a person on that plateau?

This week, the communitarians ask a different question.

The Challenge

The communitarian question

Can a theory of justice that treats persons as isolated, autonomous individuals ever make sense — when every person is already embedded in communities that shaped who they are?

Definition

What is communitarianism?

Not a single theory but a family of critiques of liberalism, united by one claim: liberals misunderstand the relationship between the individual and the community.

The community is not just an association individuals choose to join. It is the source of their identity, values, and capacity for moral reasoning.

Communitarians differ from earlier critics (Marxists, feminists) because they attack liberalism not for its outcomes but for its picture of the person.

Thinkers

Four communitarian voices

Sandel

The self is constituted by its ends, not prior to them

Taylor

Identity requires social recognition and cultural horizons

MacIntyre

Morality is rooted in traditions and narrative, not abstract principles

Walzer

Justice is local — it depends on shared social meanings

Kymlicka engages all four, but Sandel and Taylor are his primary targets.

The Debate

The liberal self (what communitarians reject)

For Rawls, the self is prior to its ends. We can always step back, examine our deepest commitments, and ask: is this really worth pursuing?

This is why the veil of ignorance works — we strip away particular identities to find principles everyone could accept.

And this is why liberals defend autonomy — because our most essential interest is in getting our beliefs about the good life right, which requires the freedom to revise them.

Sandel

Sandel: the constituted self

"We cannot regard ourselves as independent in this way without great cost to those loyalties and convictions whose moral force consists partly in the fact that living by them is inseparable from understanding ourselves as the particular persons we are." — Michael Sandel, Liberalism and the Limits of Justice

Sandel's claim: my identity as a Cypriot, a Muslim, a daughter, a member of this community is not a costume I chose. It is who I am. The liberal self, stripped of these attachments, is empty — "radically thin."

Kymlicka responds

The self prior to its ends ≠ the self without ends

Kymlicka argues Sandel confuses two things. Rawls is not saying we can perceive a self stripped of all commitments — a ghostly "substrate" behind our identities.

He is saying that no particular end is exempt from re-examination. We always have commitments, but we can always question any one of them.

Sandel himself admits that the self can be "reconstituted" — boundaries redrawn, new ends incorporated, old ones shed. But at that point, Kymlicka says, the distinction between his view and Rawls's collapses.

Taylor

Taylor: the emptiness argument

"Complete freedom would be a void in which nothing would be worth doing, nothing would deserve to count for anything." — Charles Taylor, Hegel and Modern Society

Taylor argues that total freedom to choose is meaningless unless there are already things worth choosing. Those things come from our cultural and communal horizons. Without them, we get Nietzschean nihilism — the rejection of all shared values as arbitrary.

Kymlicka responds

Freedom is not pursued for its own sake

Kymlicka agrees that freedom without purpose is empty. But that's not the liberal claim. Liberals don't say freedom is the highest value — they say our projects and commitments are the most important things in our lives.

Freedom matters because those projects matter so much that we need the ability to revise them when we discover we were wrong.

The real debate is not whether we need shared cultural resources to make choices meaningful — Kymlicka agrees we do. It is whether the community's values must be treated as authoritative horizons that individuals cannot question.

MacIntyre

MacIntyre: the embedded narrative self

"We all approach our own circumstances as bearers of a particular social identity. I am someone's son or daughter, someone else's cousin or uncle; I am a citizen of this or that city... Hence what is good for me has to be the good for one who inhabits these roles." — Alasdair MacIntyre, After Virtue

For MacIntyre, moral reasoning is not abstract principle-application. It is interpreting the narrative you are already living — discovering the story you are part of.

Kymlicka responds

MacIntyre's ambiguity

MacIntyre insists we cannot question our communal roles. But then he says: "rebellion against my identity is always one possible mode of expressing it."

If I can rebel against my inherited roles — if I can reject the expectations of my clan, tradition, or community — then I am exercising exactly the kind of revisability that Rawls defends.

Kymlicka's point: the strong claim (self-discovery replaces judgment) is implausible. The weak claim (we are shaped by communities but can question them) is attractive — but it's just liberalism.

Walzer

Walzer: justice as shared meanings

Walzer takes a different route. Instead of arguing about the self, he argues that justice is local — it depends on the shared social meanings of a particular community.

Health care is a right in a society that treats it as a communal good. It isn't in a society that treats it as a market commodity. There is no universal answer — only the meanings a community has collectively constructed.

Kymlicka's objection: shared meanings often reflect the interests of the powerful. Who gets to define "our" shared meanings? And what about dissenters within the community?

Politics

The politics of the common good

If communitarianism is right, political life should be organized around a substantive common good — shared values that define the community — rather than a neutral framework that lets individuals choose their own good.

Liberal state

Neutral between conceptions of the good. Provides a fair framework (rights, liberties, resources) within which individuals pursue their own visions.

Communitarian state

Promotes a shared conception of the good rooted in the community's traditions. The common good is not just procedural but substantive.

Critique

Whose common good?

Kymlicka identifies a deep problem. Communitarians say the community's shared practices should define justice. But:

Many communities have endorsed slavery, patriarchy, caste hierarchies, and persecution of dissenters — all as expressions of shared values.

Sandel's own communitarianism, applied consistently, could justify a community's rejection of homosexuality if that reflects its shared moral traditions.

The feminist critique from last week applies directly: whose community? Whose traditions? And who gets silenced within them?

Rawls responds

Political liberalism

Rawls tried to accommodate the communitarian critique by shifting from "comprehensive liberalism" (autonomy is good for everyone) to political liberalism (we only need an "overlapping consensus" on basic principles, not agreement on the value of autonomy).

This was meant to welcome communities that don't value individual autonomy — religious groups, traditional communities — into the liberal framework.

Kymlicka thinks this retreat fails. You cannot protect tolerance between groups without also protecting tolerance within groups — and that requires precisely the autonomy Rawls tried to downplay.

Taylor's Social Thesis

The social thesis

Taylor makes a more subtle argument. Liberals assume that individual autonomy is natural. But the capacity for autonomous choice is itself a social product — it depends on a cultural structure that provides meaningful options, education, free expression.

This is a real challenge to liberal neutrality. If the state must protect the cultural structure that makes autonomy possible, then it cannot be truly neutral — it must actively sustain certain cultural conditions.

Kymlicka accepts this point. But he distinguishes social perfectionism (society promoting conditions for autonomy) from state perfectionism (the state imposing a particular vision of the good life). Only the latter is objectionable.

Solidarity

Social unity: the communitarian's strongest point

Taylor asks: why would citizens accept the burdens of justice — redistributive taxation, mutual obligation — for strangers with radically different conceptions of the good?

Rawls and Dworkin assume a bounded political community within which citizens feel solidarity. But they never explain why citizens feel that solidarity, or what holds the community together.

Kymlicka concedes this is the communitarians' strongest insight. Liberals take for granted a sense of shared belonging that they cannot generate from their own principles.

Nations

Nationhood as the missing link

Kymlicka argues that the source of solidarity liberals take for granted is nationhood — a shared national identity, typically built through a common language, public institutions, and historical narrative.

Liberal democracies have always engaged in nation-building: standardizing languages, establishing national education systems, creating shared civic myths. This is how they generate the solidarity that makes redistribution and mutual obligation possible.

But nation-building has often been achieved through morally questionable means: suppression of minority languages, forced assimilation, exclusion of indigenous peoples.

EMU Context

Communitarianism in Northern Cyprus

This is not an abstract debate for us. Consider:

Turkish Cypriot identity — is it discovered or chosen? When the community defines its shared values, who is included? What about the diverse student body at EMU — students from dozens of countries?

Shared meanings — Walzer says justice depends on communal consensus. But Northern Cyprus has no consensus on its own political status. Whose shared meanings define justice here?

Solidarity — what binds this political community together? Ethnicity? Territory? Shared institutions? And what happens to those who don't fit?

Synthesis

The egalitarian plateau, revisited

Communitarians share the egalitarian plateau — they believe people's interests matter equally. But they define those interests differently.

Liberal view

Our essential interest is in leading a life that is genuinely good — which requires the freedom to examine and revise our beliefs about value.

Communitarian view

Our essential interest is in belonging to a community whose shared values give our lives meaning — which requires protecting those shared values.

Kymlicka's verdict: the communitarian critique has narrowed. The debate is no longer about whether individuals can question their ends — even communitarians concede they can. It becomes a debate about the social conditions that make meaningful choice possible.

Bridge

Last week meets this week

Beauvoir and Fanon asked: who constructed the subject that liberalism places on the egalitarian plateau?

The communitarians answer: the community. The self is always already shaped by communal roles, narratives, and traditions.

But Beauvoir and Fanon would push further: what if the community itself is a structure of domination? What if "shared values" are just the values of those who hold power? What if the narrative MacIntyre celebrates is a story written by the colonizer?

This tension — between community as home and community as prison — is the central unresolved question.

Kymlicka's Verdict

Can liberalism absorb communitarianism?

Kymlicka believes the communitarian critique ultimately strengthens liberalism rather than defeating it.

What communitarians get right: autonomy requires social conditions; solidarity cannot be taken for granted; cultural belonging matters for a meaningful life.

What they get wrong: treating community values as authoritative leads to the oppression of dissenters, women, and minorities. Every communitarian argument that is attractive turns out to be compatible with liberal principles.

The strong communitarian thesis collapses into liberalism. The interesting residue becomes the subject of next week: multiculturalism.

Provocation

The question that won't go away

If every communitarian argument that is attractive is also compatible with liberalism — is that a victory for liberalism, or evidence that liberalism has already absorbed so much from its critics that it is no longer recognizable as the theory Rawls proposed?
