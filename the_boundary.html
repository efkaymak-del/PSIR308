Case 1 of 7
Québec Bill 21 (Canada)

In 2019, Québec passed Bill 21 (Loi sur la laïcité de l'État), banning public-sector employees in positions of authority — teachers, police officers, judges — from wearing visible religious symbols such as hijabs, kippot, turbans, and crosses. The law invokes the notwithstanding clause to override Charter rights. Supporters frame it as protecting secular civic space; opponents call it discrimination against religious minorities, particularly Muslim women. Canada's Supreme Court is expected to hear the challenge. Québec is a national minority within Canada that is itself restricting the cultural expression of minorities within its borders — a case where Kymlicka's framework must confront a national minority using its self-government powers to impose internal restrictions on vulnerable sub-groups.

Case 2 of 7
National Minorities and Institutional Failure — Catalonia & the Kurds

Two national minorities with distinct languages, territorial bases, and historical self-governance claims — but radically different institutional contexts.

Catalonia (Spain): Catalans have their own language, an elected parliament, and a long history of self-governance. In 2017, Catalonia held a self-determination referendum. The Spanish state responded by suspending Catalan autonomy under Article 155, deploying national police to block voting, and prosecuting elected Catalan leaders — sentencing several to over a decade in prison. Spain is a consolidated EU democracy with constitutional courts and a bill of rights. Yet the institutional channels Kymlicka assumes were available — and were shut down.

Kurds (across the Middle East): Over 30 million Kurds form one of the world's largest stateless nations, spanning multiple countries. In contexts of democratic backsliding, Kurdish political parties have been repeatedly closed, elected officials imprisoned, and mother-tongue education restricted or denied. Unlike the Catalans, the Kurds have no recognised sub-state parliament or EU membership to appeal to. Where liberal-democratic institutions are weakening or absent, Kymlicka's framework loses its footing entirely.

Both groups meet Kymlicka's definition of a national minority. Neither has been accommodated through the institutional channels his theory requires. The comparison reveals that the problem is not classification — it is enforcement. Kymlicka can name the injustice but cannot compel the state to act, whether that state is a consolidated democracy or a backsliding one.

Case 3 of 7
Hijab and Abaya Ban in French Public Schools (France)

France banned conspicuous religious symbols in public schools in 2004, primarily affecting Muslim girls wearing the hijab. In 2023, the government extended the restriction to include the abaya, a loose-fitting full-length robe, on the grounds that it constituted a religious marker. France's approach is grounded in laïcité — a strict separation of religion from public institutions — and frames the ban as protecting republican civic unity. Critics argue it disproportionately targets Muslim women from immigrant-origin communities and restricts polyethnic religious expression. France's Muslim population of approximately 5–6 million is overwhelmingly descended from North African and West African immigrants who arrived from the 1960s onward. They are not a national minority with a territorial homeland in France; their claims are for religious expression and equal participation within French civic life.

Case 4 of 7
Trump's English-Only Executive Order (United States)

On March 1, 2025, President Trump signed Executive Order 14224 designating English as the sole official language of the United States. The order revoked Executive Order 13166, issued by President Clinton in 2000, which had required federal agencies to improve access to services for persons with limited English proficiency — affecting translated government documents, multilingual healthcare information, court interpretation, and voting materials. The order frames English as the foundation of national unity and civic participation. Critics warn it threatens language access for approximately 25 million US residents with limited English proficiency, disproportionately affecting Spanish-speaking, Chinese-speaking, Vietnamese-speaking, and other immigrant-origin communities. The United States has no constitutionally established official language. The affected populations are overwhelmingly polyethnic immigrant communities who are not seeking self-government or territorial autonomy but equal access to public services in their own languages.

Case 5 of 7
Roma Integration (European Union)

Approximately 6 million Roma live across the European Union, facing systemic poverty, housing segregation, employment discrimination, and educational exclusion. The EU's 2020–2030 Roma Strategic Framework acknowledged that previous integration efforts had largely failed. Roma do not hold territory, do not seek self-government, and are not recent immigrants — they have lived in Europe for centuries. This makes them extremely difficult to classify under Kymlicka's two-category framework of national minorities and polyethnic immigrant groups. They are not a national minority in Kymlicka's sense (no territorial homeland, no demand for self-government), nor are they an immigrant group seeking integration into a host society. Their claims centre on anti-discrimination, social inclusion, and recognition of cultural identity without any demand for territorial autonomy or political self-governance. The Roma case exposes the limits of a typology built around the national minority / polyethnic binary.

Case 6 of 7
Uyghur Cultural Suppression (China)

Since 2017, the Chinese government has detained an estimated one million or more Uyghurs and other Turkic Muslims in 're-education' facilities in Xinjiang. Policies include forced labour transfers, restrictions on Islamic practice, suppression of the Uyghur language in schools, mass surveillance, and forced sterilisation. The UN Office of the High Commissioner for Human Rights found in 2022 that these policies may constitute crimes against humanity. In October 2025, the UN renewed its call to end the criminalisation of Uyghur cultural and religious practices. China frames the policies as counter-terrorism and vocational training. The Uyghur claim is not for group-differentiated rights within a liberal democracy — it is for basic cultural survival under an authoritarian state that does not recognise minority rights frameworks. This case tests whether Kymlicka's theory has anything meaningful to say when its foundational assumption — a functioning liberal-democratic state — does not hold.

Case 7 of 7
The Maronites of Cyprus

The Maronite community of Cyprus has lived on the island since at least the twelfth century — they are not immigrants, not recent arrivals, and not a diaspora. They have their own language (Cypriot Maronite Arabic, or CMA, recognised by the Council of Europe in 2008), their own religious tradition (Maronite Catholic), and historically distinct villages in the north of the island, most of which were emptied after the Turkish military intervention of 1974. Yet the 1960 Constitution of the Republic of Cyprus recognised only two political communities — Greek Cypriot and Turkish Cypriot — and classified Maronites, Armenians, and Latins as 'religious groups' rather than national minorities. Each group was compelled to choose membership in one of the two dominant communities; the Maronites chose the Greek Cypriot community. Today their elected parliamentary representative sits as a non-voting observer in the House of Representatives.

The Cypriot government initially denied the very existence of CMA when the Council of Europe raised it, then claimed it was spoken only by elderly villagers in the occupied north. The bi-communal framework of the Cyprus problem — built around two and only two constituent peoples — structurally excludes Maronites, Armenians, Latins, and Roma from political recognition. Scholar Andrekos Varnava describes this as 'internal exclusion': a system that uses bi-communalism to erase the island's actual diversity. The Maronite case tests whether Kymlicka's categories — national minority or polyethnic immigrant — can account for an ancient, indigenous community that is invisible not because it is small but because the constitutional architecture has no room for it.
