The Story So Far

Utilitarianism

Maximize aggregate welfare. Problem: sacrifices individuals for the greater good.

Liberal Egalitarianism

Rawls and Dworkin: protect individuals through fair institutions. Problem: requires significant state redistribution.

Libertarianism

Nozick: self-ownership is absolute. No redistribution is legitimate. Problem: leads to morally intolerable conclusions.

Each theory emerged as a response to the failures of the previous one. Now we meet a thinker who rejects the entire framework.

The Marxist Challenge

Every theory we have studied so far operates within the same basic assumptions:

Private property exists. Markets distribute goods. The state regulates how much redistribution is fair.

Marx asks a different question entirely:

What if the system itself — capitalism — is the source of injustice, not the solution to it?

Why Marx After Nozick?

Last week, Nozick argued that voluntary exchange is always just. If you freely agree to work for someone, the arrangement is legitimate — regardless of the outcome.

Marx's response is devastating:

The worker "freely" sells her labor because the alternative is starvation. This is not freedom. This is exploitation dressed in the language of consent.

Where Nozick sees voluntary contracts, Marx sees structural coercion.

Kymlicka's Approach to Marx

Kymlicka does not present Marx as a complete alternative to liberalism. Instead, he asks:

What does Marx's critique reveal about the blind spots of every liberal theory we have studied?

Kymlicka organizes the chapter around two central Marxist claims:

1. Capitalism is inherently exploitative — even when it follows its own rules.

2. Capitalism is inherently alienating — it distorts human potential even when it creates material wealth.

Part I: Exploitation

What is wrong with capitalist exchange?

The Liberal Story of Fair Exchange

Liberals — from Locke to Rawls to Nozick — share a basic picture of economic life:

Individuals own things. They exchange freely. If the exchange is voluntary and no one is coerced, it is just.

This picture treats the economy as a series of individual transactions between equal parties.

Marx argues this picture is a fiction. It describes the surface of capitalism while hiding what happens underneath.

The Labour Theory of Value

Marx begins with a deceptively simple claim:

The value of any commodity is determined by the socially necessary labour time required to produce it.

A chair is worth more than a stick because it took more human labor to make. Value does not come from nature, from markets, or from consumer desire alone. It comes from human work.

If this is true, then something very strange is happening in capitalism.

Where Does Profit Come From?

The capitalist buys raw materials and pays workers a wage. The workers produce a commodity. The capitalist sells it for more than it cost to produce.

The difference — surplus value — is profit.

But where did that extra value come from? Not from the raw materials — they don't create value by sitting there. Not from the machines — they only transfer the value already embedded in them.

The surplus value comes from the workers producing more value than they are paid for.

This is what Marx means by exploitation. It is not a moral accusation. It is a structural description of how capitalism works.

Exploitation Is Not Cheating

This is the part that makes Marx genuinely radical:

The capitalist does not cheat the worker. The wage is "fair" by market standards. The contract is voluntary. No law is broken.

Exploitation occurs not because capitalism breaks its own rules, but because it follows them perfectly.

The worker is paid the market value of her labor power — what it costs to feed, house, and reproduce a worker. But she produces more value than that. The difference goes to the owner.

This is not a bug in capitalism. It is the system working exactly as designed.

Why Can't Workers Just Refuse?

Nozick would say: if the worker doesn't like the deal, she can walk away.

Marx's answer:

Walk away to what? The worker does not own the means of production — the factories, land, tools, technology. These are owned by the capitalist class.

The worker's only option is to sell her labor to some capitalist. She can choose which employer, but she cannot choose whether to be employed. The structure of ownership leaves no alternative.

This is why Marx calls wage labor a form of coercion — not because someone holds a gun, but because the system is designed so that one class must sell its labor to survive.

Kymlicka's Assessment of the Exploitation Argument

Kymlicka takes the exploitation critique seriously but identifies problems:

Problem 1: The Labour Theory of Value

Most economists — including many sympathetic to Marx — have abandoned the idea that labor alone determines value. Markets, scarcity, technology, and consumer preference all play a role.

Problem 2: Is All Profit Exploitation?

What about the capitalist who innovates, takes risks, or organizes production efficiently? Does their contribution create no value? Marx struggles to account for entrepreneurial labor.

But even if the specific mechanism is wrong, the underlying observation may stand: capitalism systematically transfers wealth from those who work to those who own.

Part II: Alienation

What does capitalism do to human beings?

Why Alienation Matters More Than Exploitation

Kymlicka argues that Marx's most powerful and lasting contribution is not the exploitation argument. It is the theory of alienation.

Exploitation is about the distribution of wealth. Alienation is about something deeper:

What kind of people does capitalism produce? What does it do to our capacity for meaningful, creative, fully human lives?

This shifts the question from "who gets what" to "who are we becoming."

Marx's Vision of Human Nature

Marx believed that human beings are fundamentally creative, productive, social creatures. What distinguishes us from animals is not reason alone but our capacity to transform the world through purposeful, cooperative labor.

Work — in its unalienated form — is not a burden. It is the highest expression of what it means to be human.

A carpenter who designs and builds a table, a teacher who shapes a mind, a farmer who cultivates land — these activities, when freely chosen and cooperatively organized, are what human flourishing looks like.

Capitalism, Marx argues, takes this capacity and distorts it beyond recognition.

The Four Dimensions of Alienation

Marx identifies four ways capitalism separates human beings from their own nature.

1. Alienation from the Product

The worker produces something but does not own it. The product belongs to the employer. The worker's creative output confronts her as someone else's property.

2. Alienation from the Process

The worker does not control how she works. The pace, method, and purpose of labor are dictated by the employer. Work becomes repetitive, mechanical, and meaningless.

The Four Dimensions of Alienation (continued)

3. Alienation from Other Workers

Instead of cooperating, workers compete with each other for jobs, wages, and security. Capitalism turns potential collaborators into rivals. Solidarity is structurally undermined.

4. Alienation from Species-Being

This is the deepest form. Humans are reduced to treating work as mere survival — a means to eat and pay rent — rather than as the creative, purposeful activity that defines human nature. We become alienated from what makes us human.

The tragedy is not just that workers are poor. It is that capitalism makes it impossible for anyone — workers or capitalists — to live a fully human life.

Alienation in Everyday Life

You do not need to work in a 19th-century factory to experience alienation. Consider:

The call-center worker who follows a script she did not write, selling a product she does not believe in, to meet targets set by someone she has never met.

The university student who studies not out of curiosity but to accumulate credentials for a job market that treats education as a commodity.

The gig worker who is technically "self-employed" but whose every movement is tracked and optimized by an algorithm.

Marx would say: these are not failures of the system. This is the system working.

A Local Reflection

Northern Cyprus has a large service economy built substantially on higher education and tourism. Consider:

The university worker — academic or staff — whose labor produces the institution's revenue but who has limited say in how the university is governed or how its profits are distributed.

The construction worker who builds luxury developments but could never afford to live in one.

The domestic worker from a poorer country who "freely" accepted employment but whose visa is tied to a single employer.

Marx would not ask whether these individuals signed contracts voluntarily. He would ask: what structural conditions made this the only option available to them?

The Communist Alternative

Marx's solution is not reform. It is the abolition of private ownership of the means of production.

In a communist society:

The means of production are collectively owned. No class can exploit another because no class monopolizes the tools of work.

Work is organized cooperatively. People contribute according to their abilities and receive according to their needs.

The division of labor is overcome. People are not trapped in a single role but can develop their full range of capacities.

In communist society... it is possible for me to hunt in the morning, fish in the afternoon, rear cattle in the evening, criticise after dinner, without ever becoming hunter, fisherman, herdsman or critic.

Kymlicka's Critique of the Communist Alternative

Kymlicka takes the diagnosis seriously but is skeptical of the cure.

Problem 1: The Knowledge Problem

How does a collectively owned economy decide what to produce, how much, and for whom? Markets — for all their flaws — transmit information through prices. Central planning has historically failed to replicate this efficiently.

Problem 2: The Motivation Problem

If people receive according to need regardless of contribution, what incentivizes effort, innovation, and risk-taking? Marx assumed people would work for self-realization, but historical evidence is mixed at best.

Kymlicka's Critique (continued)

Problem 3: The Freedom Problem

If the community collectively decides how production is organized, individuals who disagree have no exit option. Collective ownership can become collective coercion. Every historical attempt at communism has concentrated power in the state — the opposite of Marx's vision.

Problem 4: The Pluralism Problem

Marx assumes all people share the same vision of the good life — creative, cooperative labor. But what about those who genuinely prefer competition, solitude, spiritual contemplation, or leisure? A communist society may impose a single conception of human flourishing.

Kymlicka's verdict: Marx's critique of capitalism is more powerful than his alternative to it.

Marx vs. The Liberals: A Deeper Disagreement

The real dispute is not about redistribution. It is about what counts as a political question.

Liberals

The economy is a private sphere. The state should regulate it for fairness, but production, ownership, and work are not themselves political questions.

Justice is about the rules of distribution — how the pie is divided.

Marx

The economy is the most political sphere of all. Who owns the means of production determines everything else — law, culture, ideology, even what counts as "justice."

Justice requires asking who baked the pie and under what conditions.

What Marx Sees That Liberals Don't

Even if we reject Marx's solutions, his critique exposes genuine blind spots in liberal theory:

1. Rawls asks what principles rational agents would choose behind the veil of ignorance — but the veil hides class. Marx says class is not a variable to be abstracted away. It is the foundation of the entire system.

2. Dworkin distinguishes brute luck from option luck — but who decides what counts as a "choice" when your options are structurally constrained by class position?

3. Nozick celebrates voluntary exchange — but Marx shows that when one class owns everything, "voluntary" means "you can choose which master to serve."

Is Marx Still Relevant?

The Soviet Union collapsed. China embraced markets. Most Marxist states failed.

Does this mean Marx was wrong?

Consider: global wealth inequality has increased since 1990. The richest 1% own more than the bottom 50% combined. Gig economies have recreated conditions Marx described — workers with no security, no ownership, no control over the process of production.

Marx may have been wrong about the solution. But his description of the problem looks more accurate every year.

The philosophers have only interpreted the world. The point, however, is to change it.

— Marx, Theses on Feuerbach (1845)

The Egalitarian Plateau Revisited

Kymlicka's central argument throughout the book is that all modern political theories share a commitment to treating people as equals — the egalitarian plateau.

Marx challenges this claim:

Utilitarianism counts everyone's utility equally — but allows individuals to be sacrificed.

Rawls protects the worst off — but within a system that produces the "worst off" in the first place.

Nozick treats everyone as a self-owner — but allows self-ownership to produce destitution.

Marx says: genuine equality is impossible within capitalism. The liberal egalitarian plateau is a compromise with a fundamentally unequal system.

A Provocation

You are a student at a university that depends on international tuition fees for its revenue.

You pay for education. The university employs lecturers, administrators, cleaners, security guards. Some are paid well. Many are not. The institution produces profit — or at least aims to remain financially viable.

Marx would ask:

Who creates the value in this institution? Who captures it? Are the people who maintain the buildings, serve the food, and clean the classrooms compensated in proportion to their contribution — or in proportion to their bargaining power?

And: is your education preparing you to question this arrangement, or to take your place within it?

Connecting Forward

Feminism (Week 8)

Takes Marx's insight that "voluntary" arrangements can be structurally coercive and applies it to gender. The household is a site of unrecognized labor, invisible exploitation, and alienation — even in liberal democracies.

Critical Theory (Week 10)

Inherits Marx's method — critique of ideology, analysis of structural power — but abandons the focus on class alone. Race, gender, culture, and recognition become equally important axes of domination.

Marx opened the door. Others walked through it in directions he did not anticipate.
