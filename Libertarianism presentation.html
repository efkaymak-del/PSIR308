This Week — Libertarianism
What if redistribution itself is the injustice?

The libertarian asks a deceptively simple question:

If I own myself, how can you take what is mine?

This week we explore how far that question can go — and where it breaks down.

The Foundation: Self-Ownership

Nozick's Starting Point

Every individual has absolute ownership over their own person — their body, their mind, their talents, their labor, and everything that flows from the exercise of those capacities.

This sounds obvious. But follow the logic:

You own your body → therefore you own your labor
You own your labor → therefore you own what your labor produces
You own what you produce → therefore no one can take it without your consent
Not even for a good reason. Not even by majority vote. Not even to help the poor.

Why Should We Take This Seriously?

Nozick is not an eccentric. He is responding to a real problem in Rawls:

Rawls says:

Your natural talents are "morally arbitrary." You don't deserve them. Society can redistribute the benefits they produce.

Nozick responds:

If I don't own my talents, who does? If society owns what my talents produce, then society owns part of me. That is a form of slavery.

This is not a minor disagreement. It is a clash between two fundamentally different ideas of what it means to treat people as free and equal.

The Entitlement Theory of Justice

Nozick argues that justice is about process, not outcomes.

1
Just Acquisition
You obtained your property legitimately — you didn't steal it or take it by force.
2
Just Transfer
Anything you received came through voluntary exchange, gift, or inheritance.
3
Rectification
If either principle was violated in the past, the injustice must be corrected.

If all three are satisfied, the distribution is just — regardless of how equal or unequal it is.

Patterned vs. Historical Justice

Patterned Theories

Justice requires that the distribution of resources matches a pattern: "to each according to need," "to each equally," "to each according to merit."

Utilitarianism, Rawls, and Dworkin are all patterned theories.

Historical Theories (Nozick)

Justice depends only on how the distribution came about. If the process was just, the outcome is just. There is no pattern to maintain.

This is a completely different way of thinking about justice.

Why does this distinction matter? Because maintaining a pattern requires constant intervention in people's free choices.

The Wilt Chamberlain Argument

This is Nozick's most famous thought experiment. It attacks every patterned theory of justice simultaneously.

The Setup

Imagine your favourite distribution — call it D1. Everyone has exactly what your preferred theory says they should have. Perfect equality, or Rawlsian fairness, or whatever you like. Everyone is satisfied.

Now: Wilt Chamberlain is an extraordinary basketball player. He signs a contract: every person who comes to watch him play puts 25 cents in a special box for him. Over the season, one million people choose to watch. Chamberlain now has $250,000 more than everyone else.

Call this new distribution D2.

The Wilt Chamberlain Argument (continued)

Nozick's Question

Is D2 unjust?

Consider what happened:

D1 was just — everyone agreed it was fair
Every transaction from D1 to D2 was voluntary — no one was forced to watch basketball
Every person who paid was willing — they preferred watching Chamberlain to keeping their 25 cents
Chamberlain didn't steal, cheat, or coerce anyone

If D1 was just, and every step from D1 to D2 was voluntary, how can D2 be unjust?

To call D2 unjust, you would have to say that people are not allowed to spend their own money as they choose. You would need to continuously interfere with free exchanges to maintain your preferred pattern.

Why This Argument Is Powerful

The Wilt Chamberlain argument does not just attack one theory. It attacks the entire logic of patterned justice:

Against egalitarians: Maintaining equality requires forbidding people from making voluntary exchanges that create inequality.
Against Rawls: The difference principle must continuously override free transactions to maintain its pattern.
Against utilitarians: Even maximizing utility requires redistributing from those who have earned more through voluntary exchange.

Nozick's point: liberty upsets patterns. Any theory that requires a pattern must restrict liberty. You have to choose.

Taxation as Forced Labor

If you work 8 hours and the state takes 25% of your earnings in tax...

...Nozick argues this is morally equivalent to being forced to work 2 hours for the state.

The logic is straightforward:

If you own yourself, you own your labor
If you own your labor, you own what it produces
If the state takes part of what your labor produces without your consent, it is claiming ownership of part of your labor
Claiming ownership of someone's labor without consent is forced labor

This applies even if the tax funds healthcare, education, or poverty relief. The purpose does not change the principle.

The Minimal State

If taxation is forced labor and redistribution violates self-ownership, what role is left for the state?

Nozick's Answer: The "Night-Watchman State"

The state may only:

Protect individuals from violence and theft
Enforce contracts
Defend against external aggression

Nothing else. No public education, no welfare, no healthcare, no redistribution of any kind. Any state that does more than this violates individual rights.

The Genuine Appeal of Libertarianism

Before we critique Nozick, Kymlicka is honest about why this view attracts people. It speaks to real intuitions:

Individual dignity: You are not a tool for social engineering. Your life and labor belong to you.
Suspicion of power: States that claim the authority to redistribute can also oppress. History is full of examples.
Respect for choice: People should be free to live according to their own values, even if others disapprove.
Earned reward: If you work harder or more creatively than others, you should keep what you earn.

These intuitions are not foolish. The question is whether self-ownership can bear the weight Nozick places on it.

Kymlicka's verdict:

The intuition of self-ownership is powerful.
The theory built on it is fatally flawed.

Let's see why.

Critique 1: The Initial Acquisition Problem

Self-ownership tells us what happens after you own something. But how did anyone come to own external resources in the first place?

Nozick borrows from John Locke:

You acquire unowned property by "mixing your labor" with it. You farm unowned land, so the land becomes yours.

Kymlicka's response:

Why does mixing your labor with something make it yours? If I pour my can of tomato juice into the ocean, do I own the ocean — or have I just lost my tomato juice?

More fundamentally: once all land and resources are owned, those who arrive later have nothing. They must work for the owners on the owners' terms. Self-ownership without access to resources is an empty freedom.

The Acquisition Problem Gets Worse

Think about what this means in practice:

Generation 1 acquires all the land legitimately.

Generation 2 inherits it — a legitimate transfer.

Generation 3 inherits it again.

Generation 4 arrives with nothing. They "own themselves" but have no resources. Their only option is to sell their labor to the property-owning families.

By Nozick's own three principles, this is perfectly just. Every acquisition was legitimate. Every transfer was voluntary.

But it has produced a hereditary aristocracy — exactly the kind of entrenched inequality that self-ownership was supposed to prevent.

Kymlicka: Self-ownership without a theory of fair resource distribution is incomplete at best, dangerous at worst.

Critique 2: Formal vs. Substantive Freedom

Libertarian Freedom

Freedom = absence of coercion. No one is physically forcing you. You are free to accept or reject any offer.

The Critic's Freedom

Freedom = having real alternatives. If your only choices are exploitation or starvation, your "consent" is meaningless.

Consider this scenario: A worker "freely" accepts 12-hour days in dangerous conditions for minimal pay. No one holds a gun to her head. But the alternative is homelessness and hunger.

Is she free?

Nozick says yes — no one coerced her. Critics say no — a choice between exploitation and destitution is not a meaningful choice.

The Freedom Paradox

Kymlicka's key point:

Libertarianism begins with a commitment to individual freedom. But when followed to its logical conclusion, it can produce a world where most people have less real freedom than they would under a redistributive state.

The worker who owns herself but owns nothing else is "free" in Nozick's sense — but less free in every meaningful sense than a worker in a welfare state who has healthcare, education, and a safety net.

This is the paradox: a theory that starts with freedom can end with unfreedom.

Critique 3: Where Does Self-Ownership Lead?

If we take self-ownership as absolute and follow it rigorously:

A starving person has no enforceable claim on your surplus food
A sick child has no right to medicine you happen to own
A community cannot tax its members even for basic collective goods — roads, sanitation, defense
Inherited wealth creating a permanent aristocracy is just, as long as original acquisition was legitimate
Workers "voluntarily" accepting exploitative conditions are not owed protection

Kymlicka asks: if a theory of justice leads to conclusions that almost everyone finds morally intolerable, is the problem with our intuitions — or with the theory?

An Internal Challenge: Left-Libertarianism

Not all libertarians agree with Nozick. There is a significant internal debate:

Right-Libertarianism (Nozick)

Self-ownership extends to external property. What you acquire legitimately is yours absolutely. The state cannot redistribute.

Left-Libertarianism

Self-ownership is valid — you own your body and labor. But natural resources (land, water, minerals) belong to everyone collectively. You cannot privately appropriate what no one created.

Left-libertarians accept self-ownership but reject Nozick's theory of acquisition. This shows that even within the libertarian tradition, the initial acquisition problem is unresolved.

Connecting Back: Nozick, Rawls, and Dworkin

These three thinkers form a triangle of disagreement about the same core issue:

What is the relationship between freedom, equality, and property?

Rawls
Your talents are morally arbitrary. Justice requires redistribution so that inequalities benefit the worst off.
Dworkin
Distinguish choices from circumstances. Compensate for brute luck but respect ambition. The auction and insurance scheme operationalize this.
Nozick
You own yourself absolutely. Redistribution violates your rights. Only voluntary transfer is legitimate.

All three claim to take individual equality seriously. They disagree fundamentally on what that requires.

Libertarianism in the Real World

These are not just abstract debates. Libertarian arguments appear everywhere in contemporary politics:

Tax policy: "It's my money — the government takes it." This is Nozick's taxation-as-forced-labor argument in everyday language.
Healthcare: "Why should I pay for someone else's treatment?" Self-ownership applied to collective provision.
Property rights: Debates about land expropriation, eminent domain, and inheritance tax all invoke entitlement theory.
Labor regulation: "If someone accepts the job voluntarily, the state shouldn't interfere." Formal freedom vs. substantive freedom.
Here in Northern Cyprus: Questions about property, restitution, and who has legitimate claims to land and resources directly echo Nozick's three principles — especially rectification.

The Deep Question Kymlicka Leaves Us With

Self-ownership is intuitively powerful. Most of us feel that our bodies and our labor belong to us.

But Nozick's theory shows that if we take this intuition as absolute, it leads to conclusions most of us cannot accept.

So the question is: Is self-ownership a foundational principle of justice — or just one important value that must be balanced against others?

Two Ways to Respond to Nozick

As you think about this, notice that there are two very different ways to challenge libertarianism:

The Liberal Response (Rawls, Dworkin)

Self-ownership is important but not absolute. We can justify some redistribution because natural talents are morally arbitrary, and brute luck should be compensated. We stay within the liberal framework but limit property rights.

The Radical Response (coming in later weeks)

The entire framework — self-ownership, property, contract — is built on a history of dispossession, colonization, and gendered exclusion. You cannot fix it from within. The question is not "how much redistribution?" but "whose labor built this system and who was excluded from owning anything in the first place?"

We will return to the radical critique in Weeks 10–12. For now, hold it in mind as you test your own intuitions.

A Provocation Before We Continue

Consider this: you are a student at a university. Your education is partly funded by state subsidies, scholarships, and collective resources. The knowledge you are acquiring was produced by centuries of publicly funded research, teaching, and institutional infrastructure.

When you graduate and earn a salary, how much of that earning is truly "yours" in Nozick's sense? How much depends on a vast web of collective investment that you did not create and did not pay for?

Can you be a consistent libertarian about your own life?
