Theories on Trial

Three theories from the semester face Beauvoir and Fanon as prosecutors.

For each: does the theory survive intact, need revision, or is it fundamentally broken?

Block 2 — Theory 1

Rawls — The Veil of Ignorance

Beauvoir: The veil assumes a rational, disembodied chooser — but that "neutral" subject is already gendered. The veil hides gender as a constructed category, not just a contingent trait.

Fanon: Who designed the original position? The colonized subject does not enter it as an equal — they enter it already marked as less-than-human.

Does Rawls survive, need revision, or break?

Block 2 — Theory 2

Nozick — Self-Ownership

Fanon: The colonized body was never self-owned — it was claimed by the colonial gaze, forced labour, and racial taxonomy. Self-ownership presupposes a self that was never granted.

Beauvoir: Women's bodies have historically been property — of fathers, husbands, the state. Formal self-ownership ignores that the "self" was constructed for others' use.

Does Nozick survive, need revision, or break?

Block 2 — Theory 3

MacKinnon — Dominance Approach

Beauvoir: MacKinnon sees gender as domination — but does she go deep enough? Beauvoir argues that "woman" is not just dominated but constructed. The category itself is the product of the Other.

Fanon: MacKinnon focuses on gender but not race. Can a dominance theory that ignores colonial power explain the situation of a colonized woman?

Does MacKinnon survive, need revision, or break?
