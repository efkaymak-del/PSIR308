The Bridge from Citizenship

Last hour, we saw that just institutions require just citizens — people who participate, deliberate, and show solidarity.

But we left a question hanging: who has actually been allowed to be a citizen?

For most of political history, the answer was: property-owning men. Women were citizens in name but excluded from public life in practice — confined to the household, denied the vote, barred from property ownership, invisible in the theories we have studied.

Feminism begins here: not as an addition to liberal theory, but as an exposure of what liberal theory has systematically refused to see.

What Kind of Feminist Critique?

Kymlicka is careful to distinguish between two feminist strategies:

1. Extending Liberalism

Liberalism promises equality but has failed to deliver it for women. The solution is to apply liberal principles more consistently — extend equal rights, remove discriminatory laws, ensure equal opportunity.

2. Challenging Liberalism

The problem is not inconsistency but the framework itself. Liberalism's core concepts — the autonomous individual, the public/private divide, gender-neutral justice — are structured in ways that make gender inequality invisible.

Kymlicka engages primarily with the second, more radical strategy. The question is not "why haven't women been included?" but "what is it about liberal theory that produces exclusion?"

The Sexual Division of Labour

This is the foundation of the feminist critique as Kymlicka presents it.

Every society we know divides labour along gender lines. In most cases, women bear a disproportionate share of domestic work, care work, and emotional labour — cooking, cleaning, childcare, elder care, managing relationships, maintaining households.

This work is essential — without it, no one eats, no child is raised, no worker shows up to the office functioning.

But it is also invisible — it is unpaid, unmeasured by GDP, unrecognized by the theories of justice we have studied, and assumed to be natural rather than political.

The Public/Private Divide

Liberalism draws a line between two spheres:

The Public Sphere

Politics, the economy, the law, the market. This is where justice applies. This is where rights are enforced, where equality is demanded, where the state intervenes.

The Private Sphere

The family, the household, personal relationships, domestic life. This is protected from state interference. It is the realm of intimacy, love, and free choice.

The feminist critique: this divide is not neutral. It assigns women to the private sphere and then declares that sphere beyond the reach of justice.

What the Divide Hides

When liberalism says the family is "private," it means:

The state should not interfere in how households divide labour. If a woman does 80% of the housework, that is a private arrangement between partners — not a political issue.

But feminists ask: is it really a free choice?

If a woman "chooses" to stay home because there is no affordable childcare, because her employer penalizes mothers, because her husband earns more due to a gendered labour market, and because she was socialized from birth to see caregiving as her role — in what meaningful sense is this a choice?

The public/private divide does not protect freedom. It protects a gendered distribution of labour from political scrutiny.

The Gender-Neutral Illusion

Every theory we have studied claims to be gender-neutral. Rawls does not mention gender behind the veil of ignorance. Nozick's self-ownership applies equally to men and women. The utilitarian calculus counts every person's preference equally.

Feminists argue this neutrality is an illusion:

Rawls

Behind the veil of ignorance, rational agents design principles of justice — but they assume the family is already just. They do not ask who does the cooking, the cleaning, the childcare. The gendered division of domestic labour is invisible in the original position.

The Gender-Neutral Illusion (continued)

Nozick

Self-ownership sounds equal. But if women's domestic labour is unpaid and unrecognized, they accumulate no property. A woman who spends twenty years raising children "owns herself" but owns nothing else. When the marriage ends, Nozick's framework has nothing to say about the value she created.

Utilitarianism

The utility calculus counts preferences equally — but whose preferences are shaped by a lifetime of gendered socialization? If women have been taught to want less, to sacrifice for others, to find fulfilment in service, then counting their "preferences" equally merely reinforces the subordination that shaped those preferences.

Kymlicka's Key Concept: The Personal Is Political

This is the feminist slogan that Kymlicka takes seriously as a philosophical claim:

The personal is political.

It means: the arrangements we call "personal" — who cooks dinner, who stays home with a sick child, who manages the emotional life of a relationship, who sacrifices career advancement for family — are politically structured.

They are shaped by laws (parental leave policies, divorce law, tax codes), by markets (the gender pay gap, the cost of childcare), by culture (expectations about motherhood, masculinity, care), and by power (who gets to define what counts as "real work").

Calling them "personal" is itself a political act — it removes them from the domain of justice.

Invisible Labour

Feminist economists have attempted to measure what the liberal tradition refuses to see:

Globally, women perform approximately 75% of all unpaid care and domestic work. This amounts to an estimated 10–13 billion hours per day of unpaid labour worldwide.

If valued at minimum wage, this labour would constitute the largest sector of any national economy.

It is not counted in GDP. It is not compensated by the market. It is not addressed by Rawls's difference principle, Dworkin's auction, or Nozick's entitlement theory.

It is the foundation on which the entire "public" economy rests — and it is structurally invisible.

The Three Layers of Feminist Critique

1. Discrimination

Women are treated unequally in law, employment, and public life. This is the easiest layer to address — pass anti-discrimination laws, enforce equal pay, open institutions to women.

2. Structural Disadvantage

Even with formal equality, structures penalize women: the motherhood penalty in careers, the absence of affordable childcare, the gendered division of domestic labour. These are not individual prejudices but systemic patterns.

3. The Construction of Gender

The deepest layer: gender itself is politically constructed. What it means to be a "woman" or a "man" — the roles, expectations, desires, and limitations — is produced by social institutions, not by nature. Justice requires interrogating the construction, not just redistributing within it.

Susan Moller Okin: Justice in the Family

Kymlicka draws heavily on Okin's argument that the family must be subject to principles of justice, not exempted from them.

Okin's challenge to Rawls: if the veil of ignorance is supposed to eliminate morally arbitrary advantages, why doesn't it eliminate gender? If you don't know whether you'll be a man or a woman, you would design institutions that don't penalize either.

This means: publicly funded childcare, parental leave for all genders, equal division of domestic responsibilities as a matter of justice — not charity, not personal preference, but a requirement of fairness.

Rawls himself acknowledged late in his career that Okin's critique was largely correct. The family cannot be outside the scope of justice.

The Care Critique

Some feminists — notably Carol Gilligan and Nel Noddings — argue that the problem goes deeper than applying liberal principles to the family.

The entire liberal framework is built on a masculine model of the self: the autonomous, independent, rational individual who enters society to negotiate rights and exchange goods.

But human beings are not autonomous atoms. We are born dependent. We spend much of our lives giving or receiving care. We are constituted by relationships — not despite them but through them.

A theory of justice built on the fiction of independent individuals will always undervalue the work of those who make human dependence survivable — and that work is overwhelmingly done by women.

What Would a Feminist Theory of Justice Look Like?

Kymlicka does not commit to a single feminist alternative, but several directions emerge:

1. Abolish the Public/Private Divide

Subject the family to the same principles of justice that govern the economy and the state. If the difference principle applies to wages, it should apply to the distribution of domestic labour.

2. Recognize and Compensate Care Work

If care work is essential to the functioning of society, it must be valued — through wages, social insurance, pension credits, and public services that socialize its costs.

A Feminist Theory of Justice (continued)

3. Restructure Institutions

Workplaces, schools, and governments must be redesigned around the reality that all citizens — not just women — have care responsibilities. Flexible work, universal childcare, parental leave for all genders.

4. Interrogate the Construction of Gender

Justice requires not just redistributing resources between men and women but questioning why "men" and "women" are assigned different roles, different expectations, and different forms of labour in the first place.

This last point is the most radical: it suggests that gender justice is not about making the existing system fairer but about transforming the categories themselves.

A Local Reflection

Consider the university you attend. Who teaches? Who cleans? Who cooks in the cafeteria? Who manages the dormitories? Who does the emotional labour of advising, mentoring, and supporting students?

Now consider your own household — whether that is a family home, a shared flat, or a dormitory room. Who cleans? Who cooks? Who organizes? Who remembers birthdays, schedules appointments, manages the emotional temperature of the space?

Is this division natural? Or is it politically structured — shaped by expectations about gender, by economic incentives, by cultural norms that assign different kinds of work to different kinds of people?

The feminist critique does not ask you to feel guilty. It asks you to see what has been made invisible.

Connecting Back to Our Theories

Marx

Marx saw exploitation in the factory but not in the household. Feminists extended his method: if surplus value is extracted from wage workers, what about the unpaid domestic labour that makes wage work possible? Who exploits the woman who raises the next generation of workers for free?

Citizenship Theory

If active citizenship requires time, energy, and freedom from domestic burden, then the gendered division of labour is a direct barrier to women's civic participation. The "private" sphere determines who can be a citizen in the "public" sphere.

The feminist critique reveals that every theory on the egalitarian plateau has a gender-shaped blind spot — not because the theorists were individually sexist, but because the framework itself was built on a distinction (public/private) that renders half of human labour invisible.

Objections and Tensions

Kymlicka acknowledges several tensions within the feminist critique:

Choice versus Structure: If a woman genuinely prefers full-time caregiving, is it unjust? Feminists disagree. Some say preferences formed under patriarchy cannot be truly free. Others say denying women's stated preferences is itself paternalistic.

Difference versus Equality: Should feminism aim for identical treatment of men and women, or for recognition that women's distinct experiences (pregnancy, breastfeeding, disproportionate care responsibilities) require different treatment to achieve substantive equality?

Intersectionality: The feminist critique as Kymlicka presents it focuses primarily on gender. But gender intersects with class, race, nationality, and disability. A wealthy white woman's experience of the public/private divide differs radically from a domestic worker's.
