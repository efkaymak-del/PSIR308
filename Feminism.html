Feminism and Sexual Equality

Kymlicka, Chapter 9

Week 8 — Eastern Mediterranean University — Spring 2026

Where We Are

Every theory so far has asked: what do individuals owe each other, and what does the state owe its citizens?

Utilitarians said: maximize welfare. Rawls said: protect the worst off. Nozick said: protect self-ownership. Marx said: abolish the system that produces inequality. Citizenship theory said: none of this works unless citizens actively sustain just institutions.

Feminism asks a question that cuts across all of these: do any of these theories actually see gender?

The Challenge

Kymlicka's feminism chapter is not about one feminist argument. It is about a family of competing diagnoses — liberal feminism, radical feminism, and socialist feminism — each identifying a different root cause of gender inequality.

These approaches agree that women are systematically disadvantaged. They disagree about why, and therefore about what justice requires.

Kymlicka uses their disagreements to test a deeper question: can liberalism survive the feminist critique, or does it need to be replaced?

Three Feminist Approaches

Liberal feminism: The problem is exclusion. Women have been denied the rights, opportunities, and protections that liberal theory promises to all individuals. The solution is to extend liberal principles consistently — equal access, anti-discrimination law, reproductive autonomy.

Radical feminism (MacKinnon): The problem is domination. Gender inequality is not a failure to apply liberal principles but a system of male power embedded in culture, sexuality, and institutions. Extending liberal rights within a patriarchal structure changes nothing fundamental.

Socialist feminism: The problem is the intersection of capitalism and patriarchy. Women's unpaid domestic and care labour subsidizes the market economy. Gender oppression cannot be separated from class oppression.

Liberal Feminism: The Case for Extension

Liberal feminists argue that liberalism already contains the resources to address gender inequality — it simply has not applied them consistently.

Autonomy, equal opportunity, and anti-discrimination are powerful principles. The problem is that historically, "individual" meant "male individual." Women were excluded from property ownership, voting, education, and professional life — not because liberalism demanded it, but because liberals failed to follow their own logic.

On this view, the solution is more liberalism, not less — extend rights, remove barriers, enforce equal treatment.

Kymlicka takes this position seriously. He does not dismiss it.

The Problem with Extension

Critics — including radical and socialist feminists — argue that extending liberal principles is necessary but insufficient.

Why? Because formal equality operates against a background of structural inequality. Giving women the same legal rights as men does not address the fact that women enter the labour market carrying a disproportionate burden of unpaid domestic work, face workplaces designed around male life patterns, and make "choices" shaped by gendered socialization from birth.

This is the point where the feminist critique begins to echo Kymlicka's earlier discussions of Rawls and Dworkin — the gap between formal and substantive equality is not new. What is new is the claim that gender is where liberalism's blind spot is deepest.

The Public/Private Divide

Every liberal theory we have studied draws a line between the public sphere (where justice applies) and the private sphere (where individuals are free to live as they choose).

Feminists argue that this line is the single most important mechanism through which gender inequality is maintained.

The family, sexuality, domestic labour, emotional care — all are classified as "private" and therefore placed beyond the reach of justice. But these are precisely the domains where women's subordination is produced and reproduced daily.

"The personal is political" — this is not a slogan. It is a theoretical claim: the private sphere is a political construction that serves political functions.

What the Theories Miss

Utilitarianism counts everyone's preferences equally — but if women's preferences have been shaped by subordination, aggregating them simply ratifies the status quo.

Rawls designs just institutions from behind the veil of ignorance — but his original position does not clearly account for gender, and he treats the family inconsistently (sometimes as part of the basic structure, sometimes not).

Nozick defends voluntary exchange — but consent is meaningless when one party's options have been systematically narrowed by gendered expectations and economic dependency.

Marx diagnoses exploitation through surplus value — but focuses on wage labour and undertheorizes the unpaid labour that makes wage labour possible.

MacKinnon's Dominance Approach

Catharine MacKinnon argues that gender inequality is not discrimination — it is domination.

The difference between these two framings is fundamental. Discrimination means applying different rules to men and women. Domination means that the rules themselves — including the rules that appear neutral — are constructed through and for male power.

"Virtually every quality that distinguishes men from women is already affirmatively compensated in this society. Men's physiology defines most sports, their needs define auto and health insurance coverage, their socially designed biographies define workplace expectations and successful career patterns."

On MacKinnon's account, "gender neutrality" is not neutral at all. It takes the male standard as the human standard and then measures women against it.

Sexuality as Hierarchy

MacKinnon's most radical claim is that sexuality itself is socially constructed through male power.

This is not a claim about individual relationships. It is a structural argument: the cultural scripts that define desire, attractiveness, and sexual norms are produced within a system of male dominance. Women's subordination is eroticized — made to appear natural, desirable, and chosen.

If this is true, then the liberal appeal to "consent" and "free choice" in sexual matters is deeply compromised. How can consent be meaningful when the preferences themselves are shaped by the system of domination?

This is the hardest question in the chapter.

The Pornography Debate

MacKinnon and Andrea Dworkin proposed anti-pornography ordinances that would allow women to bring civil suits against pornographers on the grounds that pornography is not speech but a practice of sex discrimination.

Their argument: pornography does not merely depict women's subordination — it constructs it. It normalizes the eroticization of dominance and submission, shaping how men see women and how women see themselves.

This puts feminism in direct conflict with one of liberalism's core commitments: freedom of expression.

Liberals — including many feminist liberals — objected that censorship is a dangerous tool that historically has been used against women and minorities, not for them. The cure, they argued, is more speech, not less.

Kymlicka uses this debate to show how the dominance approach pushes liberalism to its limits.

The Equality vs. Difference Debate

Within feminism itself, a major fault line runs between two positions.

The equality approach: Justice requires treating men and women the same. Any legal distinction based on sex risks reinforcing stereotypes and trapping women in traditional roles. Equal treatment, equal standards, equal access.

The difference approach: Justice requires recognizing that men and women have different needs — pregnancy, breastfeeding, disproportionate caregiving responsibilities. Treating unequal situations equally produces unequal outcomes. Genuine equality requires accommodation, not identical treatment.

MacKinnon rejects both positions. She argues that the debate itself is framed by male dominance: "equality" means conforming to the male standard, while "difference" means accepting a subordinate identity defined by that standard. The real question is not sameness or difference but power.

Gender Neutrality Is Not Neutral

Consider a workplace that offers no parental leave, evaluates employees on uninterrupted career progression, and rewards long hours and geographic mobility. This workplace has no gender-specific rules. It is formally neutral.

But it is designed around a worker who has no caregiving responsibilities — which, in practice, means a worker whose domestic labour is performed by someone else, overwhelmingly a woman.

The feminist critique of gender neutrality is that neutrality, applied to an unequal background, reproduces the inequality. This echoes what we learned from Rawls (formal vs. substantive equality) and from Dworkin (choices vs. circumstances) — but feminism argues that neither Rawls nor Dworkin followed the insight far enough into the family and the gendered division of labour.

Socialist Feminism: Gender Meets Class

Socialist feminists argue that gender oppression cannot be understood apart from capitalism. Women's unpaid domestic labour is not just a cultural norm — it is an economic subsidy to the market economy.

A worker who arrives at the office fed, clothed, emotionally supported, and with children cared for is a worker whose employer has received enormous value — produced by someone who was not paid.

This connects directly to what we studied in the Marxism module: surplus value, exploitation, and the gap between who produces value and who captures it. Socialist feminism extends Marx's analysis to the household and asks: if unpaid domestic labour is exploitation, why did Marx himself ignore it?

The answer, socialist feminists argue, is that Marx reproduced the public/private divide he claimed to critique.

The Invisible Labour Data

Global time-use studies consistently show that women perform approximately 9–10 hours of invisible labour per day (domestic work, care work, emotional labour, household administration) compared to approximately 3–4 hours for men — a gap of roughly six hours.

This gap persists even when women work full-time in paid employment. It persists across income levels, education levels, and cultural contexts. It narrows in some countries and widens in others, but it never closes.

Every theory we have studied this semester has a response to this data. None of them — until feminism — made it central.

Your Invisible Labour Tracker results are part of this picture. We will return to them in Wednesday's workshop.

Okin: Justice in the Family

Susan Moller Okin argued that Rawls's theory of justice, properly applied, demands justice within the family — not just in public institutions.

If the family is part of the basic structure of society (and Rawls sometimes concedes it is), then the difference principle applies: inequalities within the family are just only if they benefit the least advantaged member.

The gendered division of domestic labour fails this test. It systematically disadvantages women — reducing their earnings, career advancement, economic independence, and exit options — without compensating them.

Okin's move is to take liberalism at its word and push it into the domain it tried to protect as "private." This is the liberal feminist strategy at its most powerful — and its most uncomfortable for liberals.

Can Liberalism Survive?

This is where Kymlicka's own position becomes important.

MacKinnon says no: liberalism is structurally patriarchal. Its categories — consent, choice, neutrality, the public/private divide — are not innocent tools that happen to have been misapplied. They are instruments of male power.

Liberal feminists say yes: liberalism's core commitments to autonomy, equality, and individual rights are powerful enough to accommodate the feminist critique. The problem is incomplete application, not the principles themselves.

Kymlicka sides — cautiously — with the liberal feminists. He argues that a truly egalitarian liberalism must integrate feminist insights about structural power, gendered socialization, and the political nature of the private sphere. Liberalism does not need to be abandoned. It needs to be substantially revised.

But he takes MacKinnon's challenge seriously. The revision required is not cosmetic.

Local Reflection: EMU and Northern Cyprus

Think about the gendered structures around you. Who cleans university dormitories and faculty housing? Who manages households while partners study or work? Who are the domestic workers in Famagusta — and what citizenship status do they hold?

Many domestic workers in Northern Cyprus are migrant women from Southeast Asia and Sub-Saharan Africa. They perform invisible labour not only as women but as non-citizens — outside the protections of labour law, citizenship rights, and political voice.

The feminist critique intersects here with citizenship theory: who counts as a member of the community whose labour sustains it? And does MacKinnon's dominance framework, or the liberal feminist framework, or the socialist feminist framework best explain their situation?

The Egalitarian Plateau — Again

Kymlicka's thesis throughout the book is that all major traditions share a commitment to treating persons as equals — the egalitarian plateau.

Feminism is the strongest test of that thesis. If liberalism, libertarianism, utilitarianism, and Marxism all claim to treat persons as equals but all fail to see gendered subordination, then either the plateau is shallower than Kymlicka thinks, or the theories need deeper revision than they have so far undertaken.

Kymlicka believes the revision is possible. MacKinnon believes it is not. Your job — in the workshop, in the brief, and in the rest of this course — is to decide where you stand.
