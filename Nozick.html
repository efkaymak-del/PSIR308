This Week — Libertarianism
What if redistribution itself is the injustice?

The libertarian asks a deceptively simple question:

If I own myself,
how can you take what is mine?

This week we explore how far that question can go — and where it breaks down.

The Foundation: Self-Ownership

Nozick's Starting Point

Every individual has absolute ownership over their own person — their body, their mind, their talents, their labor, and everything that flows from the exercise of those capacities.

This sounds obvious. But follow the logic:

You own your body → therefore you own your labor
You own your labor → therefore you own what your labor produces
You own what you produce → therefore no one can take it without your consent
Not even for a good reason. Not even by majority vote. Not even to help the poor.

Why Should We Take This Seriously?

Nozick is not an eccentric. He is responding to a real problem in Rawls:

Rawls says:

Your natural talents are "morally arbitrary." You don't deserve them. Society can redistribute the benefits they produce.

Nozick responds:

If I don't own my talents, who does? If society owns what my talents produce, then society owns part of me. That is a form of slavery.

This is not a minor disagreement. It is a clash between two fundamentally different ideas of what it means to treat people as free and equal.

The Entitlement Theory of Justice

Nozick argues that justice is about process, not outcomes.

1
Just Acquisition
You obtained your property legitimately — you didn't steal it or take it by force.
2
Just Transfer
Anything you received came through voluntary exchange, gift, or inheritance.
3
Rectification
If either principle was violated in the past, the injustice must be corrected.

If all three are satisfied, the distribution is just — regardless of how equal or unequal it is.

Two Kinds of Justice Theory

Patterned Theories

Justice requires that distribution matches a formula: "to each according to need," "to each equally," "to each according to merit."

Utilitarianism, Rawls, and Dworkin are all patterned theories.

Historical Theories (Nozick)

Justice depends only on how the distribution came about. If the process was just, the outcome is just. There is no correct pattern.

This is a completely different way of thinking about justice.

Why does this matter? Because maintaining any pattern requires constant intervention in people's free choices.

The Wilt Chamberlain Argument

This is Nozick's most famous thought experiment. It attacks every patterned theory simultaneously.

The Setup

Imagine your favourite distribution — call it D1. Everyone has exactly what your preferred theory says they should have. Perfect equality, Rawlsian fairness, whatever you like.

Now: Wilt Chamberlain is an extraordinary basketball player. He signs a contract: every person who watches him play puts 25 cents in a special box for him. Over the season, one million people choose to watch.

Chamberlain now has $250,000 more than everyone else. Call this new distribution D2.

Is D2 Unjust?

Consider what happened:

D1 was just — everyone agreed it was fair
Every transaction from D1 to D2 was voluntary
Every person who paid chose to — they preferred watching basketball to keeping their 25 cents
Chamberlain didn't steal, cheat, or coerce anyone

If D1 was just, and every step from D1 to D2 was voluntary, how can D2 be unjust?

To restore D1, you would have to forbid people from spending their own money as they choose. You would need to continuously interfere with free exchanges.

Liberty upsets patterns.

Any theory that requires maintaining a distributional pattern must restrict individual freedom to do so.

This applies to egalitarians, to Rawls, to utilitarians — to everyone who thinks justice requires a particular outcome.

You have to choose: liberty or pattern. You cannot have both.

Taxation as Forced Labor

If you work 8 hours and the state takes 25% of your earnings in tax...

...Nozick argues this is morally equivalent to being forced to work 2 hours for the state.

The logic:

You own yourself → you own your labor
You own your labor → you own what it produces
The state takes part of what your labor produces without your consent
Claiming ownership of someone's labor without consent = forced labor

This applies even if the tax funds healthcare, education, or poverty relief.

The Minimal State

If taxation is forced labor, what role remains for the state?

The "Night-Watchman State"

The state may only:

Protect individuals from violence and theft
Enforce contracts
Defend against external aggression

Nothing else. No public education, no welfare, no healthcare, no redistribution of any kind.

Any state that does more than this is, by Nozick's logic, violating individual rights.

The Genuine Appeal

Before we critique Nozick, Kymlicka is honest about why this view attracts people:

Individual dignity: You are not a tool for social engineering. Your life and labor belong to you.
Suspicion of power: States that claim the authority to redistribute can also oppress.
Respect for choice: People should live according to their own values.
Earned reward: If you work harder or more creatively, you should keep what you earn.

These intuitions are not foolish. The question is whether self-ownership can bear the weight Nozick places on it.

Kymlicka's verdict:

The intuition of self-ownership is powerful.

The theory built on it is fatally flawed.

Critique 1: The Acquisition Problem

Self-ownership tells us what happens after you own something. But how did anyone come to own external resources in the first place?

Nozick borrows from Locke:

You acquire unowned property by "mixing your labor" with it. You farm unowned land, so the land becomes yours.

Kymlicka's response:

Why does mixing your labor with something make it yours? If I pour my can of tomato juice into the ocean, do I now own the ocean — or have I just lost my tomato juice?

More fundamentally: once all land is owned, those who arrive later have nothing. They must work for the owners on the owners' terms.

The Acquisition Problem Gets Worse

Generation 1 acquires all the land legitimately.

Generation 2 inherits it — a legitimate transfer.

Generation 3 inherits again.

Generation 4 arrives with nothing. They "own themselves" but have no resources. Their only option is to sell their labor to the property-owning families.

By Nozick's own three principles, this is perfectly just.

But it has produced a hereditary aristocracy — exactly the kind of entrenched inequality that self-ownership was supposed to prevent.

Can the Lockean Proviso Save Nozick?

Locke added a condition to acquisition: you may appropriate resources only if you leave "enough and as good" for others.

Nozick's weakened version:

Your appropriation is legitimate as long as it does not make others worse off than they would have been without any private property at all.

Kymlicka argues this is far too weak. The baseline — life without any property system — is so miserable that almost any appropriation "passes" the test. A feudal lord who lets serfs use some of his land could argue they are better off than in a state of nature.

The proviso was supposed to limit acquisition. In practice, it legitimizes almost everything.

Critique 2: Formal vs. Substantive Freedom

Libertarian Freedom

Freedom = absence of coercion. No one is physically forcing you. You are free to accept or refuse any offer.

The Critic's Freedom

Freedom = having real alternatives. If your only choices are exploitation or starvation, your "consent" is meaningless.

A worker "freely" accepts 12-hour days in dangerous conditions for minimal pay. No one holds a gun to her head. But the alternative is destitution.

Nozick says she is free. Critics say she is not.

The Freedom Paradox

Libertarianism begins with a commitment to individual freedom.

But when followed to its logical conclusion, it can produce a world where most people have less real freedom than they would under a redistributive state.

The worker who owns herself but owns nothing else is "free" in Nozick's sense — but less free in every practical sense than a worker with healthcare, education, and a safety net.

A theory that starts with freedom can end with unfreedom. This is the central paradox of libertarianism.

Critique 3: Morally Intolerable Conclusions

If self-ownership is absolute:

A starving person has no enforceable claim on your surplus food
A sick child has no right to medicine you happen to own
A community cannot tax its members even for roads or sanitation
Inherited wealth creating a permanent aristocracy is just
Workers accepting exploitative conditions are not owed protection

If a theory of justice leads to conclusions almost everyone finds morally intolerable, is the problem with our intuitions — or with the theory?

Does Self-Ownership Even Work on Its Own Terms?

Kymlicka pushes further. Even the core intuition is less solid than it appears:

Ownership implies transferability. If you own your body the way you own property, can you sell yourself into slavery? Nozick's framework struggles to explain why not.
Ownership implies exclusion. But your "self" is partly constituted by relationships, education, language, and culture — all of which are collective. What part of you is purely "yours"?
The body is not like property. You didn't make, buy, or earn your body. You were born with it. In what sense did you "acquire" it?

The metaphor of self-as-property may be fundamentally misleading.

Left-Libertarianism: An Internal Challenge

Right-Libertarianism (Nozick)

Self-ownership extends to external property. What you acquire legitimately is yours absolutely.

Left-Libertarianism

Self-ownership is valid — you own your body and labor. But natural resources belong to everyone collectively. You cannot privately appropriate what no one created.

Left-libertarians accept self-ownership but reject Nozick's theory of acquisition.

This shows that even within libertarianism, the acquisition problem is the fault line. Self-ownership alone does not tell you who owns what.

The Triangle of Disagreement

Rawls
Your talents are morally arbitrary. Justice requires redistribution so inequalities benefit the worst off.
Dworkin
Distinguish choices from circumstances. Compensate brute luck, respect ambition.
Nozick
You own yourself absolutely. Only voluntary transfer is legitimate. No redistribution.

All three claim to take individual equality seriously. They disagree fundamentally on what that requires. Notice that each emerged in response to the weaknesses of the others.

Libertarianism in the Real World

These are not abstract debates. Libertarian arguments appear daily:

Tax policy: "It's my money — the government takes it." This is Nozick in everyday language.
Healthcare: "Why should I pay for someone else's treatment?"
Labor regulation: "If someone accepts the job voluntarily, the state shouldn't interfere."
Inheritance: "I earned this for my children — the state has no right to it."
Northern Cyprus: Questions about property, restitution, and legitimate claims to land directly echo Nozick's three principles — especially rectification.

A Local Application: Rectification

Nozick's third principle — rectification — is actually the most radical and least discussed part of his theory.

If property was unjustly acquired or unjustly transferred at any point in history, the current distribution is unjust and must be corrected.

Nozick himself acknowledged that applying rectification to the real world — where almost all existing property distributions are built on histories of conquest, colonization, and dispossession — could require massive redistribution.

This is ironic: the theory most associated with opposing redistribution contains within it a principle that could demand the most radical redistribution of all.

Think about what rectification would mean in this part of the world.

A Provocation

You are a student at a university. Your education is partly funded by state subsidies, scholarships, and collective resources. The knowledge you are acquiring was produced by centuries of publicly funded research, teaching, and institutional infrastructure.

When you graduate and earn a salary, how much of that earning is truly "yours" in Nozick's sense? How much depends on a vast web of collective investment that you did not create and did not pay for?

Can you be a consistent libertarian about your own life?

The Question Kymlicka Leaves Us With

Self-ownership is intuitively powerful. Most of us feel that our bodies and our labor belong to us.

But Nozick's theory shows that if we take this intuition as absolute, it leads to conclusions most of us cannot accept.

Is self-ownership a foundational principle of justice — or just one important value that must be balanced against others?

Two Ways to Respond to Nozick

The Liberal Response (Rawls, Dworkin)

Self-ownership matters but is not absolute. Some redistribution is justified because talents are morally arbitrary and brute luck should be compensated. We limit property rights within the liberal framework.

The Radical Response (Weeks 10–12)

The entire framework — self-ownership, property, contract — is built on histories of dispossession, colonization, and exclusion. The question is not "how much redistribution?" but "whose labor built this system and who was excluded from owning anything?"

We will return to the radical critique later. For now, hold it in mind.
