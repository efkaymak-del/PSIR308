Critical Theory

Simone de Beauvoir & Frantz Fanon

Week 10 — Eastern Mediterranean University — Spring 2026

The Story So Far

Kymlicka has guided us through a series of traditions — utilitarianism, liberal egalitarianism, libertarianism, Marxism, citizenship, feminism — and argued that they share a deep commitment to moral equality: the egalitarian plateau.

Each tradition disagrees about what equality requires. But all of them, Kymlicka claims, begin from the same premise: that every person's life matters equally.

Last week, feminism tested that claim. MacKinnon argued that liberalism's categories — consent, choice, neutrality — are not innocent tools but instruments of male power. Kymlicka responded that liberalism can absorb the critique.

This week, we meet two thinkers who go further. They argue that the problem is not what liberalism fails to see. The problem is what liberalism is.

Why Beauvoir and Fanon Together?

Simone de Beauvoir (1908–1986) wrote about gender. Frantz Fanon (1925–1961) wrote about race and colonialism. They are often taught in separate courses.

We read them together because they share a single, devastating insight: oppression is not just a distribution problem. It is an identity problem.

Liberal theory asks: are resources distributed fairly? Are rights protected equally? Beauvoir and Fanon ask a prior question: who gets to count as a full human being in the first place? And who decided?

If some people are constructed as less-than-human before the distribution even begins, then redistributing resources within the existing framework changes nothing fundamental.

The Concept: The Other

Both Beauvoir and Fanon build on a concept from existentialist philosophy: the Other.

To be "the Other" is to be defined not by yourself but by someone else — to exist as an object in another's world rather than as a subject in your own. The Other is seen, classified, and known — but does not get to see, classify, or know in return.

Beauvoir: women are constructed as the Other of men. Man is the Subject, the default, the universal. Woman is the deviation, the particular, the secondary.

Fanon: Black people are constructed as the Other of whiteness. The colonized person exists in the colonizer's gaze — reduced to a body, a skin colour, a set of projections.

This is not a metaphor. It is a description of how power works at the level of identity itself.

Beauvoir: "One Is Not Born a Woman"

"One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman. No biological, psychological, or economic fate determines the figure that the human female presents in society; it is civilization as a whole that produces this creature."

This is arguably the most important sentence in twentieth-century feminist philosophy.

Beauvoir's claim is that gender is not a natural fact but a social construction. "Woman" is not a biological category that determines social roles. It is a set of meanings, expectations, and limitations imposed on female bodies by a civilization organized around male subjectivity.

Notice how this radicalizes the feminist debate from last week. Liberal feminists said: extend rights to women. MacKinnon said: the system is built on male dominance. Beauvoir says: the category "woman" itself is a product of that system.

Beauvoir: Immanence and Transcendence

Beauvoir draws on existentialist philosophy to describe two modes of human existence.

Transcendence: the capacity to project oneself into the future, to create, to act, to define oneself through freely chosen projects. This is what existentialists mean by authentic human existence — you are not a fixed thing but an ongoing becoming.

Immanence: being trapped in repetition, maintenance, and biological function. Cleaning, feeding, caring, reproducing — necessary labour that sustains life but does not create anything new. It is existence without a project.

Beauvoir argues that women have been systematically confined to immanence — to the domestic, the bodily, the repetitive — while men have claimed transcendence as their domain. The division is not natural. It is enforced.

And here is the critical point: liberalism does not see this division because it happens before the liberal subject enters the public sphere.

Beauvoir and the Failure of Liberal Freedom

Liberalism promises freedom — autonomy, self-determination, the right to choose your own life. Beauvoir does not reject this promise. She argues that it is systematically withheld from women.

The withholding does not happen through law (though it has, historically). It happens through the construction of femininity itself. A girl is taught from birth that her value lies in being desired, in nurturing, in self-sacrifice. By the time she reaches adulthood and is formally "free," her subjectivity has already been shaped by a system that directs her toward immanence.

This is why Beauvoir goes beyond liberal feminism. Giving women legal rights does not undo a lifetime of constructed identity. And it is why she goes beyond MacKinnon: the problem is not just male dominance in institutions but the construction of gendered subjects who experience their subordination as natural.

Beauvoir's Challenge to Kymlicka

Kymlicka's framework assumes that individuals have preferences, and that justice is about distributing resources in a way that respects those preferences fairly.

Beauvoir's challenge: what if the preferences themselves are products of oppression?

This is the same question MacKinnon raised — but Beauvoir grounds it in a richer philosophical framework. For Beauvoir, the issue is not just that women's preferences are distorted by patriarchy. It is that the entire structure of subjectivity — who you understand yourself to be, what you believe you want, what you imagine is possible for you — is constructed within a system that assigns you to immanence or transcendence based on your sex.

If this is true, then Kymlicka's egalitarian plateau has a problem it cannot solve from within: it treats as given the very subjects that need to be liberated.

From Gender to Race: Enter Fanon

Beauvoir showed that gender is constructed through the logic of the Other. Fanon shows that race operates through the same logic — but with a violence and totality that gender oppression, while devastating, does not fully capture.

Frantz Fanon was born in Martinique, a French colony. He studied psychiatry in France, then joined the Algerian revolution against French colonial rule. He died at 36.

His two major works — Black Skin, White Masks (1952) and The Wretched of the Earth (1961) — are among the most powerful texts in anti-colonial thought. They are also direct challenges to everything Kymlicka's liberal framework assumes.

Fanon: The Colonial Gaze

"Look, a Negro!" — and then the circle tightened. I was responsible at the same time for my body, for my race, for my ancestors. I subjected myself to an objective examination, I discovered my blackness, my ethnic characteristics; and I was battered down by tom-toms, cannibalism, intellectual deficiency, fetishism, racial defects."

This passage from Black Skin, White Masks describes the moment of racial interpellation — the moment when the Black person is reduced, in the white gaze, to a body, a colour, a set of stereotypes.

Fanon's point is not that racism involves negative attitudes. It is that the colonial system produces a form of subjectivity in which the colonized person is denied the status of a full human being. You are not seen as a person who happens to be Black. You are seen as Black — and everything follows from that.

Fanon: The Zone of Non-Being

Fanon describes the experience of the colonized subject as inhabiting a "zone of non-being" — a space outside the boundaries of recognized humanity.

This is not a metaphor for poverty or political exclusion. It is an ontological claim: the colonial system does not merely exploit or oppress the colonized. It denies them being itself. The colonized person is not an exploited worker (Marx), not a citizen denied rights (liberalism), not even a dominated subject (MacKinnon). The colonized person is not fully recognized as human.

If Beauvoir showed that women are constructed as the Other, Fanon shows that the colonized are constructed as something even further from subjectivity — as objects, as animals, as things to be used.

Every theory we have studied this semester assumes that the subjects of justice are already recognized as persons. Fanon asks: what happens when they are not?

The Egalitarian Plateau from the Zone of Non-Being

Kymlicka's egalitarian plateau assumes that all major traditions begin from a shared commitment to moral equality — that every person's life matters equally.

Fanon's response: who counts as a person?

The liberal tradition emerged in Europe during the same centuries that Europe was colonizing Africa, Asia, and the Americas. The Enlightenment philosophers who articulated universal human rights simultaneously justified slavery, colonial rule, and racial hierarchy. Locke owned shares in the Royal African Company. Kant wrote a racial hierarchy. Mill defended colonialism as benevolent tutelage.

Fanon does not treat this as hypocrisy — a failure to live up to liberal principles. He treats it as constitutive: the liberal subject was defined against the colonial Other. Universalism was built on exclusion.

Fanon: The Psychology of Colonialism

As a psychiatrist, Fanon was uniquely positioned to describe what colonialism does to the mind.

The colonized person internalizes the colonizer's gaze. They learn to see themselves through the eyes of the oppressor — to measure their worth by the colonizer's standards, to desire the colonizer's culture, to despise their own.

"For the black man there is only one destiny. And it is white."

This internalized colonialism is not a personal psychological problem. It is a structural effect of a system that defines humanity in the colonizer's image. The colonized person who straightens their hair, who adopts the colonizer's language, who seeks recognition within the colonizer's institutions, is not making a free choice. They are performing the only form of subjectivity the system makes available.

Compare this with Beauvoir: women perform femininity not because they choose it but because the system constructs no alternative subjectivity. The parallel is structural.

Fanon: Violence and Liberation

Fanon's most controversial argument — developed in The Wretched of the Earth — is that decolonization requires violence.

This is not a tactical claim (violence works). It is a philosophical claim: the colonial system has so thoroughly dehumanized the colonized that only an act of radical rupture can restore their subjectivity. Violence is the moment when the colonized person refuses to be an object — when they act as a subject for the first time.

Every liberal theory we have studied would condemn this argument. Rawls would insist on institutional reform. Nozick would defend property rights. Utilitarians would calculate the costs. Even Marx, who theorized revolution, imagined it as a collective, historical process — not an existential act of self-creation.

Fanon's point is that all of these responses assume the colonized person is already recognized as a participant in the political order. They are not. You cannot reform a system from within when the system does not recognize you as existing within it.

The Pre-Political

Here is the concept that unites Beauvoir and Fanon and distinguishes them from every thinker we have studied so far.

Liberal theory, Marxism, even MacKinnon's radical feminism — all of these operate at the level of politics: institutions, rights, distributions, power structures. They assume that the subjects who enter the political arena are already constituted as subjects — as persons with identities, preferences, and the capacity for political agency.

Beauvoir and Fanon argue that oppression operates at a pre-political level: it constructs the subjects themselves. Before you can ask "what does justice require?" you have to ask "who has been allowed to become a full human being?" and "whose subjectivity has been constructed by the system that claims to liberate them?"

This is the deepest challenge to Kymlicka's framework. The egalitarian plateau assumes equal moral persons. Beauvoir and Fanon ask how those persons were made — and unmade.

What Rawls Cannot See

Behind the veil of ignorance, Rawls asks: what principles would you choose if you did not know your position in society?

Beauvoir's objection: the veil does not remove gendered subjectivity. The person behind the veil is already constituted — already shaped by a lifetime of gendered socialization. You cannot think from nowhere. You think from where you have been made.

Fanon's objection: the veil assumes that everyone behind it is recognized as a person capable of rational deliberation. But the colonial system has produced subjects who have been denied rationality itself — who have been classified as sub-human, as incapable of self-governance. The original position excludes them not by design but by assumption.

The veil of ignorance, designed to ensure fairness, reproduces the exclusions it was meant to eliminate — because it cannot reach the pre-political level where those exclusions are produced.

What Nozick Cannot See

Nozick's self-ownership thesis: you own yourself, your labour, and the products of your labour. No one may take them without consent.

Fanon's response: the colonized person does not own themselves. Their body has been claimed by the colonial system — as labour, as spectacle, as object. The slave's body belonged to the master. The colonial subject's identity belongs to the colonizer. Self-ownership presupposes a self that has not been expropriated.

Beauvoir's response: the woman who has been constructed as immanence — as body, as caretaker, as object of desire — does not experience self-ownership in any meaningful sense. Her "self" has been produced by a system that assigned her a function before she could choose one.

Self-ownership is a conclusion, not a starting point. It requires a prior liberation that Nozick's theory cannot account for.

What Marx Cannot See

Marx diagnosed exploitation as the extraction of surplus value from workers who are formally free but structurally coerced. Fanon admired Marx but argued that the colonial situation is not reducible to class.

In the colony, the division is not between bourgeoisie and proletariat. It is between colonizer and colonized — and the line is drawn by race, not class. A white worker in Algeria had more in common with a white factory owner than with an Arab worker. Race cuts across class.

Beauvoir made a parallel argument about gender: Marx's analysis of exploitation focuses on wage labour and ignores the unpaid labour of women. The category of "worker" is gendered — it assumes a male subject whose reproductive needs are met by someone else.

Both thinkers push Marxism to confront what it shares with liberalism: a universalism that is not as universal as it claims.

Northern Cyprus Through Fanon's Lens

Fanon wrote about Algeria, Martinique, and Sub-Saharan Africa. But his analysis travels.

Consider Northern Cyprus. A territory shaped by colonial rule (Ottoman, British), ethnic partition (1974), and ongoing non-recognition. Greek Cypriots lost property in the north; Turkish Cypriots lost property in the south. Migrant workers from the Global South perform the labour that sustains the economy but are excluded from political membership.

Fanon would ask: who is the Subject and who is the Other in this context? Whose history is told as the official narrative? Whose labour is visible and whose is invisible? Who is recognized as a full member of the political community and who exists in a zone of non-being — present but unrecognized?

These are not abstract questions in Famagusta.

Beauvoir in the Classroom

Beauvoir would ask us to turn the lens on ourselves.

Who speaks in this classroom and who stays silent? Whose theoretical intuitions are treated as serious contributions and whose are dismissed? When we ran the simulations — the veil of ignorance, the self-ownership dilemma, the alienation audit, the invisible labour tracker — whose experiences were centred and whose were invisible?

The feminist workshop last week asked whether the personal is political. Beauvoir and Fanon take this further: the personal is pre-political. Your sense of who you are, what you deserve, and what you can imagine for yourself has been shaped by systems of power that operated on you before you ever walked into a political philosophy classroom.

Does knowing this change anything? That is an open question.

Can Liberalism Respond?

Kymlicka would argue yes — cautiously. Liberal egalitarianism can expand its conception of the basic structure to include the pre-political: education, media, cultural norms, family structures. Rawls's theory can be revised to account for the construction of subjects, not just the distribution of resources.

Beauvoir might partially agree: she was not anti-liberal. She believed in freedom. But she insisted that freedom requires material and existential conditions that liberalism has not yet secured — and cannot secure without confronting how subjects are made.

Fanon would be more sceptical. The liberal framework emerged from the same civilization that produced colonialism. Its universalism was always particular — European, white, male. Reform from within reproduces the structure. Only rupture — intellectual, cultural, and sometimes physical — can create the conditions for genuine freedom.

The egalitarian plateau either stretches to accommodate this critique, or it breaks.

Beauvoir and Fanon: Convergence

Despite their different contexts, Beauvoir and Fanon converge on five claims.

1. Oppression operates at the level of identity, not just distribution. It constructs who you are, not just what you have.

2. The oppressed internalize the oppressor's gaze. Women perform femininity; the colonized perform whiteness. Both mistake the performance for nature.

3. Liberal categories — choice, consent, autonomy, rights — presuppose a free subject. But the subject has been produced by the system they are supposed to be free within.

4. Liberation requires more than redistribution. It requires the creation of new forms of subjectivity — new ways of being human that the current system cannot imagine.

5. Philosophy is not neutral. It is produced within power structures and serves their interests — unless it is consciously turned against them.

The Pre-Political Lens

We now have a concept that will carry through the rest of the course: the pre-political.

Every theory we study from this point forward — communitarianism, multiculturalism — can be tested against the pre-political question: does this theory address how subjects are constructed before they enter the political arena, or does it take existing identities, preferences, and communities as given?

Communitarians (next week) will argue that identity is formed in communities — and that liberalism's abstract individualism ignores this. Fanon and Beauvoir would ask: which communities? Formed by whom? In whose interest?

Multiculturalists will argue for group rights and cultural recognition. Fanon would ask: does recognition within the existing system liberate the colonized, or does it domesticate them?

The pre-political lens does not give you answers. It gives you a question that will not go away.

Objections and Limits

The agency objection: If subjects are entirely constructed by oppressive systems, how can they resist? Beauvoir and Fanon both insist on the possibility of liberation — but the tension between structural determination and individual agency is real and unresolved.

The violence objection: Fanon's endorsement of revolutionary violence is his most criticized position. Can liberation be achieved without it? Beauvoir thought so — through consciousness-raising and existential freedom. Gandhi thought so. Mandela changed his mind on the question more than once.

The essentialism objection: Does Fanon replace racial hierarchy with racial essentialism? Does Beauvoir's analysis of "woman" account for differences among women — race, class, sexuality? Later thinkers (Audre Lorde, bell hooks, Kimberlé Crenshaw) pushed this critique further.

The constructive objection: Beauvoir and Fanon are powerful diagnosticians. But what do they build? What institutions, what principles, what political order follows from their critique? This is the hardest question.

The Egalitarian Plateau — Under Maximum Pressure

We have now arrived at the point of maximum tension in the course.

Kymlicka's thesis: all traditions share a commitment to moral equality. They disagree about what it means, not about whether it matters.

Beauvoir and Fanon's counter-thesis: the "equality" that liberal, libertarian, utilitarian, and even Marxist traditions claim to champion was constructed by excluding women, colonized peoples, and racialized groups from full humanity. The plateau is not a shared foundation. It is a shared illusion — or at best, an aspiration that has never been realized and cannot be realized within the frameworks that produced it.

Your final project asks you to take a position on this question. Today's lecture is the material that makes the question hardest.

A Provocation

You are a student at a university in a territory that most of the world does not recognize. You study political philosophy written by Europeans about European problems. You run simulations designed to test European theories of justice. You are asked to write about equality in a language that is not your first language, in an institution modelled on Western higher education, in a city shaped by colonialism and partition.

Fanon would ask: whose knowledge counts here? Beauvoir would ask: what subjectivity has this institution produced in you? Both would ask: is the fact that you are asking these questions a sign of liberation, or a sign that the system is working exactly as designed?

You do not need to answer this now. But you need to sit with it.
