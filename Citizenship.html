PSIR 308: Contemporary Political Philosophy

Citizenship Theory

Kymlicka, Chapter 7

Week 7 — Eastern Mediterranean University — Spring 2026

Where We Are in the Course

Every theory we have studied so far asks the same question: what do individuals owe each other, and what does the state owe its citizens?

Utilitarians answered: maximize welfare. Rawls answered: protect the worst off. Nozick answered: protect self-ownership. Marx answered: abolish the system that produces inequality.

All of these theories focus on what citizens receive — rights, resources, protections, freedoms.

Citizenship theory asks a different question entirely.

The New Question

What kind of citizens does a just society need — and what must citizens do, not just receive, for justice to work?

This is a shift from citizenship-as-rights to citizenship-as-practice.

It is not enough to design just institutions. Someone has to sustain them.

Why This Chapter Exists

Kymlicka notes that for most of the 20th century, political philosophers focused almost entirely on institutions and rights — the "structure" of justice.

By the 1990s, a growing consensus emerged: just institutions are not self-sustaining. They depend on citizens who possess certain qualities — civic virtues — that no theory of rights alone can produce.

The welfare state requires citizens willing to pay taxes honestly. Democracy requires citizens willing to inform themselves and participate. Multiculturalism requires citizens willing to engage across difference.

Without these virtues, even perfectly designed institutions decay.

Citizenship-as-Rights: The Liberal Model

The classic liberal view — running from Locke through Rawls — treats citizenship primarily as a legal status that confers rights.

You are a citizen. Therefore you have rights: to vote, to free speech, to due process, to equal treatment. The state protects these rights. You go about your private life.

This is sometimes called "passive citizenship" or the "private citizen" model. The citizen is a rights-bearer, not an active participant.

Kymlicka argues this model is incomplete — not wrong, but dangerously insufficient.

The Problem with Passive Citizenship

What Passive Citizenship Assumes

Citizens will obey the law. Citizens will pay taxes. Citizens will show up to vote occasionally. The state handles the rest.

What Actually Happens

Voter turnout declines. Tax evasion grows. Public discourse coarsens. Citizens treat the state as a service provider and themselves as consumers. Trust erodes. Institutions hollow out.

The diagnosis: rights without responsibility produces citizens who free-ride on institutions they do not maintain.

Citizenship-as-Practice: The Republican Revival

Civic republicanism — drawing on Aristotle, Machiavelli, and Rousseau — argues that citizenship is not a status but an activity.

To be a citizen is to participate in collective self-governance. It requires showing up, deliberating, compromising, and taking responsibility for the common good.

Freedom is not the absence of interference. Freedom is self-governance — and self-governance requires active citizens.

This tradition sees political participation not as a burden but as the highest expression of human freedom.

What Are Civic Virtues?

Kymlicka identifies several qualities that citizenship theorists argue a just society requires:

Public Reasonableness

The willingness to justify your political positions in terms that others can accept — not just "because my religion says so" or "because it benefits me" but through reasons accessible to all citizens regardless of their private commitments.

Civic Engagement

Active participation in democratic life — voting, deliberating, serving on juries, attending public meetings, staying informed about policy.

Civic Virtues (continued)

Tolerance and Mutual Respect

The capacity to live alongside people whose values, beliefs, and ways of life differ fundamentally from your own — and to treat them as equal citizens despite the disagreement.

Solidarity and Trust

A sense of shared fate with fellow citizens — the willingness to sacrifice some personal advantage for the collective good, to pay taxes that fund services you may never use, to support institutions that protect people unlike yourself.

Without these virtues, Kymlicka argues, no theory of justice — liberal, libertarian, or otherwise — can function in practice.

The Liberal Dilemma

Here is the central tension Kymlicka identifies:

Liberalism is committed to neutrality — the state should not impose a particular vision of the good life. People should be free to pursue their own values.

But civic virtues are a particular vision of the good life — one that values participation, reasonableness, and solidarity over private withdrawal, dogmatism, and self-interest.

Can a liberal state promote civic virtues without betraying its commitment to neutrality?

If it cannot, liberalism may be structurally incapable of producing the citizens it needs to survive.

Where Do Civic Virtues Come From?

This is the practical question that citizenship theorists wrestle with. If the state cannot directly teach values without becoming illiberal, where do civic virtues develop?

The Family

Children learn trust, cooperation, and fairness at home — or they don't. The family is the first school of citizenship.

Civil Society

Voluntary associations — religious groups, unions, sports clubs, charities — teach people to cooperate, negotiate, and take responsibility for something larger than themselves.

The Market

Even economic participation teaches punctuality, reliability, and the capacity to work with strangers. But it also teaches competition and self-interest.

Education and Citizenship

Of all the institutions that produce civic virtues, education is the most contested.

Should public schools teach democratic values — tolerance, critical thinking, respect for diversity? Most citizenship theorists say yes.

But this creates conflicts. Parents who hold conservative religious views may object to their children learning values that contradict their faith. Communities that reject liberal norms may see civic education as cultural imperialism.

Kymlicka acknowledges that civic education is essential but cannot pretend it is neutral. Teaching children to question authority, to respect diversity, and to reason publicly is a substantive moral commitment — one that conflicts with some citizens' deepest convictions.

A Local Reflection

Consider the situation at EMU and in Northern Cyprus more broadly:

A university campus with students from dozens of countries, speaking different languages, holding different political and religious commitments. The surrounding society is itself a product of contested sovereignty and unresolved political division.

What does citizenship mean here? Is it a legal status — your passport, your residency permit? Or is it a practice — how you engage with the community, how you treat people who differ from you, whether you participate in collective life?

Many of you hold citizenship in one country while living in another. Some of you hold contested citizenship. The gap between formal citizenship and lived citizenship is something you experience daily.

Critiques of Citizenship Theory

Kymlicka does not present citizenship theory uncritically. Several objections emerge:

The Coercion Problem

If the state promotes civic virtues, it risks becoming paternalistic — telling citizens how to think and live. This is precisely what liberalism was designed to prevent.

The Exclusion Problem

Historically, "civic virtue" has been defined by dominant groups. Active citizenship was reserved for property-owning men. Women, minorities, and the poor were told they lacked the virtues necessary for full participation. Citizenship theory can reproduce these exclusions if it is not careful about whose virtues count.

Critiques (continued)

The Structural Problem

Citizenship theory focuses on individual virtues — be more engaged, be more tolerant, be more reasonable. But what if the barriers to participation are structural? Poverty, discrimination, lack of education, precarious work — these are not failures of civic virtue but failures of justice. Telling a single mother working two jobs to be a more active citizen misses the point.

Marx would add: civic virtue within an exploitative system is window dressing. You cannot have genuine solidarity among citizens whose economic relationship is one of exploitation.

Connecting Back to Our Theories

Rawls

Rawls assumed citizens would develop a "sense of justice" — a willingness to support fair institutions. Citizenship theory asks: where does this sense come from? Rawls never fully answered.

Nozick

Libertarianism has no theory of civic virtue. If citizenship is merely a contract for mutual protection, there is no obligation to participate, deliberate, or show solidarity. The minimal state produces minimal citizens.

Marx

Marx would argue that civic virtue is impossible under capitalism — genuine solidarity requires abolishing the class structure that divides citizens into exploiters and exploited.

Utilitarianism

Utilitarianism values civic participation only instrumentally — if it maximizes welfare. But what if apathy maximizes aggregate satisfaction? Utilitarianism cannot explain why participation matters intrinsically.

Citizenship and the Egalitarian Plateau

Citizenship theory introduces a dimension that the egalitarian plateau overlooks:

Every theory on the plateau asks what people are owed. Citizenship theory asks what people owe.

If the egalitarian plateau is about treating people as equals, citizenship theory adds: equals must also treat each other as equals — through their daily practices, their public reasoning, their willingness to engage across difference.

This is not a competing theory of justice. It is a reminder that justice is not only about institutional design but about the character of the people who inhabit those institutions.

A Provocation

You are enrolled in a course on political philosophy at a university in a divided society. You have studied theories of justice, equality, freedom, and exploitation.

Has this course made you a better citizen?

Not a better student — a better citizen. Has it changed how you engage with people who disagree with you? Has it made you more willing to participate in collective life? Has it given you reasons to care about justice that go beyond passing the exam?

If the answer is no, citizenship theory would say: the course has failed, regardless of your grade.

If the answer is yes — where did that change come from? The readings? The workshops? The simulations? The arguments with classmates?

The Key Takeaway

Just institutions require just citizens. And producing just citizens is itself a political project — one that no theory of rights alone can accomplish.

Hold this thought as we move into the feminist critique. If citizenship requires certain virtues, and those virtues are cultivated in institutions — families, schools, workplaces — then the question of who controls those institutions, and whose labor sustains them, becomes the most important political question of all.
